CH 06 Surgery in the Tropics (Continued)
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Primary source of tuberculous infection in the first question (TB spreading by ascending infection from urethra to kidney)N-03
✓ dBy ascending infection from urethra to kidney — This is the answer shown in the book for the EXCEPT question. Primary TB sources include direct inhalation, bovine (milk/gut), and lymphatic/haematogenous spread. Ascending urethral spread to the kidney is actually SECONDARY renal TB, not a primary source — hence the "EXCEPT" answer.
Q2Lepra type I reaction occurs commonly inN-04
✓ aBorderline leprosy — Type I (Reversal) reaction is a delayed hypersensitivity reaction (CMI-mediated) occurring in BORDERLINE leprosy (BT, BB, BL). It does NOT occur in polar forms (TT or LL). Type II (ENL) occurs in LL and BL.
Q3Germinal layer of hydatid cyst isN-04
✓ cEndocyst (inner germinal layer) — Hydatid cyst has 3 layers: (1) Pericyst — host-derived fibrous layer, (2) Ectocyst — outer laminated membrane (acellular), (3) Endocyst — inner germinal (nucleated) layer that produces brood capsules, scolices, and daughter cysts.
Q4Increased incidence of cholangiocarcinoma is seen in all EXCEPTN-08
✓ aHydatid cyst of liver — Hydatid cyst does NOT increase risk of cholangiocarcinoma. Risk factors for cholangiocarcinoma: Primary sclerosing cholangitis, liver flukes (Clonorchis sinensis, Opisthorchis), choledochal cysts, hepatolithiasis, ulcerative colitis, polycystic liver disease.
Q5Loeffler's syndrome is caused byM-11
✓ aAscaris lumbricoides — Loeffler's syndrome = transient pulmonary eosinophilia caused by larval migration through the lungs. Most common cause = Ascaris lumbricoides larvae passing through the pulmonary vasculature. Also caused by hookworm, Strongyloides.
Q6All of the following are intermediate hosts in hydatid cyst EXCEPTS-13
✓ cDogs — Dogs are the DEFINITIVE host (harbour adult tapeworm Echinococcus granulosus in their intestine). Intermediate hosts (harbour larval stage/hydatid cysts): sheep, cattle, pigs, horses, camels, and humans (accidental intermediate host).
Q7Leonine facies is seen in which type of leprosyS-18
✓ cLepromatous leprosy — Leonine facies (lion-like face) = thickening and nodulation of facial skin (forehead, cheeks, ears, nose) due to diffuse infiltration by M. leprae. Characteristic of lepromatous leprosy (low CMI, high bacterial load).

CH 07 Principles of Laparoscopic and Robotic Surgery
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Minimally Access Surgical procedures are becoming more popular becauseM-03
✓ dAll of the above — MAS advantages: less painful and disfiguring (smaller incisions), fewer complications (less wound infections, adhesions, hernias), can be performed in high-risk patients (less physiologic stress). Hence all three options are correct.
Q2The gas used in laparoscopic surgery to create pneumoperitoneumN-09, S-13
✓ dCO₂ (Carbon dioxide) — CO₂ is the gas of choice for laparoscopic insufflation because: highly soluble in blood (rapidly absorbed and excreted via lungs), non-combustible (safe with electrocautery), non-toxic, cheap, and readily available.
Q3During laparoscopic procedure, following gas is used for insufflating peritoneal cavityS-12
✓ aCO₂ — Same as Q2. CO₂ is the universal gas for laparoscopic pneumoperitoneum. O₂ and N₂O are flammable/support combustion (dangerous with diathermy).
Q4Advantages of carbon dioxide in laparoscopic surgery are all EXCEPTW-15
✓ aNon-irritant — CO₂ IS irritant to the peritoneum. It dissolves in peritoneal fluid to form carbonic acid (H₂CO₃), which causes referred shoulder tip pain (phrenic nerve irritation) post-operatively. True advantages of CO₂: non-flammable, non-toxic, rapidly absorbed.
Mnemonic for CO₂ advantages: Non-flammable, Rapidly absorbed, Inexpensive, Non-explosive. Disadvantage: IRRITANT to peritoneum.
Q5Gas most suited for laparoscopy isW-17
✓ cCO₂ — Consistent with Q2 and Q3. CO₂ remains the gold standard gas for laparoscopic surgery worldwide.

CH 08 Principles of Paediatric Surgery
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Which statement is NOT true regarding Hirschsprung's disease?M-03
✓ bRectal biopsy shows absent nerve fibres — This is actually TRUE (it's the gold standard diagnostic finding — absence of ganglion cells in Meissner's and Auerbach's plexuses). The question asks for what is NOT true. However, the statement is correctly stated, so this is a tricky question. All options (a, c, d) are true. Rectal biopsy shows ABSENT GANGLION CELLS (not absent nerve fibres per se) — nerve fibres are increased (hypertrophied cholinergic fibres). The subtle error is "absent nerve fibres" vs "absent ganglion cells."
Q2Duhamel operation is done forN-09
✓ dHirschsprung's disease — Duhamel operation = retrorectal transanal pull-through. One of three operations for Hirschsprung's disease: (1) Swenson (original), (2) Duhamel (retrorectal), (3) Soave (endorectal pull-through). All remove the aganglionic segment.
Q3Complications of Ramstedt's operation includeN-09
✓ dAll of the above — Complications of Ramstedt's pyloromyotomy: gastroenteritis (post-op feeding problems), post-operative pyrexia, wound disruption (duodenal perforation is also feared), wound infection. All listed complications can occur.
Q4The most common malignant neoplasm of infancy isN-10
✓ bNeuroblastoma — Most common solid extracranial malignant tumour in infancy/childhood. Arises from neural crest cells (adrenal medulla, sympathetic ganglia). Presents with abdominal mass, opsoclonus-myoclonus, raccoon eyes. Elevated urinary catecholamines (VMA, HVA).
Q5Ladd's procedure is done forM-11
✓ cMalrotation of gut — Ladd's procedure: (1) Division of Ladd's bands (peritoneal bands causing duodenal obstruction), (2) Widening of the mesentery, (3) Appendicectomy, (4) Placing small bowel on right and colon on left. Corrects midgut malrotation.
Q6Ramstedt's operation is done inS-15
✓ cInfantile Hypertrophic Pyloric Stenosis (IHPS) — Ramstedt pyloromyotomy: splitting the hypertrophied pyloric muscle longitudinally down to mucosa without entering the lumen. Pre-op: correct hypokalemic, hypochloraemic metabolic alkalosis first.
Q7Congenital hypertrophic pyloric stenosis is associated withW-15
✓ bHypokalemic alkalosis — IHPS causes non-bilious projectile vomiting → loss of HCl (H⁺ + Cl⁻) → metabolic alkalosis. Paradoxical aciduria: kidneys retain H⁺ to preserve K⁺ → hypokalaemia. Classic: hypokalemic, hypochloraemic metabolic alkalosis.
Q8Double bubble sign is seen inW-15
✓ aDuodenal atresia — Double bubble sign on AXR: two air-fluid levels — one in the stomach and one in the dilated first part of duodenum, with absence of gas distally. Classic finding of duodenal atresia. Associated with Down syndrome (30%).
Q9Commonest indication for liver transplantation in infant isS-17
✓ dBiliary atresia — Biliary atresia (progressive obliteration of bile ducts) is the single most common indication for liver transplantation in children/infants, accounting for ~50% of paediatric liver transplants. Kasai portoenterostomy is initial treatment; transplant for failures.
Q10In congenital pyloric stenosis the defect usually lies inS-17
✓ bCircular muscle fibre — IHPS involves hypertrophy and hyperplasia of the CIRCULAR muscle layer of the pylorus, causing a palpable "olive-shaped" mass. The longitudinal muscle is relatively unaffected. Ramstedt's pyloromyotomy splits only the circular muscle fibres.
Q11Ramstedt's operation is forW-17
✓ dCongenital hypertrophic pyloric stenosis — Repeat confirmation of Q6. Ramstedt = pyloromyotomy for IHPS.
Q12Duhamel's operation is done for which condition?S-18
✓ bHirschsprung's disease — Repeat of Q2. Duhamel operation = retrorectal pull-through for Hirschsprung's disease.
Q13Acute pancreatitis causesS-18
✓ dAll of the above — Complications of acute pancreatitis include: pancreatic necrosis (local), pleural effusion (usually left-sided, due to pancreatic duct-pleural fistula or diaphragmatic inflammation), and ascites (pancreatic ascites from ductal disruption). All three can occur.

CH 09 Principles of Oncology
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Plexiform neurofibromatosis affects more commonlyM-03
✓ dPeripheral nerve — Plexiform neurofibromas involve peripheral nerves along their entire length, causing a "bag of worms" sensation. Pathognomonic of NF-1 (von Recklinghausen's disease). They diffusely involve nerve plexuses and peripheral nerves.
Q2Plexiform neurofibromatosis commonly affects following cranial nerveN-03
✓ b5th Trigeminal nerve — Among cranial nerves, the trigeminal nerve (CN V) is most commonly affected by plexiform neurofibroma, causing facial disfigurement. NF-2 (bilateral acoustic neuromas) affects CN VIII, but NF-1 plexiform type preferentially involves CN V.
Q3The commonest soft tissue tumour in AIDS isN-04
✓ bLymphoma — Non-Hodgkin's lymphoma is the commonest soft tissue/systemic tumour in AIDS. Kaposi's sarcoma (caused by HHV-8) is the most common tumour overall in AIDS (including skin), but among soft tissue tumours specifically, lymphoma predominates. (Note: Some sources say Kaposi's is the most common AIDS-defining malignancy — either a or b depending on framing; book answer = b.)
Q4Which tumour responds best to radiation?N-04
✓ cWell-differentiated carcinoma — Well-differentiated tumours are most radiosensitive. The Bergonié-Tribondeau law: rapidly dividing, undifferentiated cells are MORE radiosensitive. Paradoxically, well-differentiated tumours (seminoma, follicular thyroid) respond well to radiation clinically. Most radiosensitive: seminoma, lymphoma, small cell carcinoma.
Classic radiosensitive tumours mnemonic — "6 L's": Lymphoma, Leukaemia, Liver (hepatoma), Lung (small cell), Larynx (glottis), and testicular Lesions (seminoma).
Q5Type of radiation used in radiotherapyN-04
✓ dIonising radiation — Radiotherapy uses ionising radiation (X-rays, gamma rays, electron beams, proton beams) to damage DNA of tumour cells, causing double-strand breaks and cell death. Non-ionising radiation (UV, infrared) does NOT have enough energy to ionise atoms.
Q6The gene implicated in Peutz-Jeghers syndrome isM-11
✓ dSTK11 (LKB1) — Peutz-Jeghers syndrome: autosomal dominant, STK11/LKB1 gene mutation (serine-threonine kinase). Features: hamartomatous GI polyps + mucocutaneous pigmentation (lips, buccal mucosa, fingers). MSH1/MSH2/PMS1 are Lynch syndrome (HNPCC) genes.
Q7Which of the following is a sign of malignant transformation of a tumour?S-14
✓ dAll of the above — Signs of malignant transformation: fixity to surrounding structures (local invasion), regional lymph node involvement (lymphatic spread), and distant metastasis. Any one or combination of these indicates malignancy.
Q8Gardner's syndrome is characterised byS-17
✓ cPolyposis colon + osteoma + epidermal inclusion cyst + fibrous tumour of skin — Gardner's syndrome = APC gene mutation. Triad: (1) FAP (colonic polyposis), (2) Osteomas (mandible, skull), (3) Soft tissue tumours (epidermal cysts, desmoid tumours, fibromas). High risk of colorectal cancer.
Q9All are recognised tumour markers EXCEPTW-19
✓ dNone (i.e., all are recognised tumour markers) — Beta HCG (choriocarcinoma, testicular tumours), Beta-2 microglobulin (multiple myeloma, lymphoma), Alpha-fetoprotein (hepatocellular carcinoma, germ cell tumours) are ALL valid tumour markers. Hence "None" is the EXCEPT answer.

CH 12 Patient Safety
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Basal cell carcinoma is divided macroscopically into all EXCEPTS-19
✓ cNodulocystic — BCC macroscopic types: nodular (most common, pearly edge with telangiectasia), pigmented, superficial, morphoeic/sclerosing, and fungating. "Nodulocystic" is not a standard macroscopic classification. Nodular ± cystic changes describes microscopic pattern, not a separate macroscopic type.
CH 13 Diagnostic Imaging
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1In which patient is MRI contraindicated?M-03
✓ aPatient with bullet injury — Metallic foreign bodies (bullets, shrapnel) are absolute contraindications for MRI — the strong magnetic field can cause migration of ferromagnetic fragments, causing tissue injury or haemorrhage. Renal failure and unconscious patients can have MRI with precautions.
Q2Absolute contraindication for MRI include all of the following EXCEPTN-10
✓ b1st trimester of pregnancy — Pregnancy (especially 1st trimester) is a RELATIVE contraindication, not absolute. Absolute contraindications to MRI: pacemakers, cochlear implants, metallic heart valves (older types), ferromagnetic aneurysm clips, metallic orbital foreign bodies.
Q3Absolute contraindication of MRI isW-11
✓ aCochlear implants — Cochlear implants (electronic devices with ferromagnetic components) are absolute contraindications to MRI. The magnetic field can damage the implant and cause pain/injury. Intracranial lesions, bone/joint lesions, and soft tissue lesions are actually INDICATIONS for MRI.
Q4Which of the following is used to generate X-rays?W-11
✓ cTungsten — X-ray tubes contain a tungsten target (anode). When high-speed electrons strike the tungsten target, they decelerate and produce X-rays (Bremsstrahlung radiation). Tungsten's high melting point (3422°C) and high atomic number (Z=74) make it ideal.
Q5One CT Abdomen is equivalent to how many chest X-rays?W-13
✓ d500 chest X-rays — CT abdomen radiation dose ≈ 8–10 mSv, equivalent to approximately 400–500 chest X-rays (each ≈0.02 mSv). This highlights the significant radiation burden of CT scans. ALARA principle (As Low As Reasonably Achievable) should guide imaging decisions.
Q6FAST focuses on all the following areas EXCEPTW-16
✓ dRetrocolic — FAST (Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma) examines 4 areas: (1) Pericardial (subxiphoid view), (2) Perihepatic/Morrison's pouch (right upper quadrant), (3) Perisplenic (left upper quadrant), (4) Pelvic (pouch of Douglas). "Retrocolic" is NOT a standard FAST window.
Q7FAST stands forS-19
✓ cFocussed Assessment with Sonography for Trauma — Standard ATLS terminology. FAST is a bedside ultrasound exam used in trauma to detect free fluid (haemoperitoneum, haemopericardium) rapidly. Extended FAST (eFAST) also assesses bilateral pleural spaces for haemo/pneumothorax.

CH 15 Tissue Diagnosis
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Which one of the following is NOT a tumour marker?M-03
✓ aInterleukin II (IL-2) — IL-2 is a cytokine (immunomodulator), NOT a tumour marker. Valid tumour markers: CEA (colorectal, pancreatic), AFP (hepatocellular carcinoma, germ cell tumours), HCG/βHCG (choriocarcinoma, testicular tumours), PSA, CA-125, CA 19-9.
Q2Granuloma isN-08
✓ cTumour-like proliferation of inflammatory tissue — A granuloma is a focal collection of epithelioid macrophages (± Langhans giant cells, lymphocytes, plasma cells) — a chronic inflammatory reaction. It is NOT a true tumour. Causes: TB, sarcoidosis, Crohn's, leprosy, foreign body.
Q3Regarding CEA, which is FALSE?N-10, W-13
✓ dElevated only when there is hepatic metastasis — CEA is FALSELY stated as only elevated in hepatic metastasis. In fact, CEA is elevated in primary colorectal cancer (ANY stage), pancreatic cancer, gastric cancer, lung cancer, and many benign conditions (smoking, cirrhosis). It is a prognostic marker monitored post-resection for recurrence.

CH 17 Anaesthesia and Pain Relief
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Maximum tourniquet time for the upper limb isM-04
✓ d120 minutes — Maximum safe tourniquet time: Upper limb = 2 hours (120 min), Lower limb = 1.5 hours (90 min). Beyond this, risk of ischaemia-reperfusion injury, nerve damage, and Volkmann's contracture. Tourniquet pressure: systolic BP + 50–75 mmHg (upper limb), systolic + 75–100 mmHg (lower limb).
Q2Local anaesthetic drugs are not effective in presence of infection because ofN-04
✓ cDecreased pH — Infected tissues have ACIDIC pH (due to bacterial metabolism and lactic acid). Local anaesthetics (weak bases, pKa 7.6–8.9) exist in ionised form in acidic pH and CANNOT penetrate nerve membranes. The unionised lipophilic form is needed for neural penetration. Hence LA fails in abscesses.
Q3Longest acting local anaesthetic drug isN-04
✓ dBupivacaine (Marcaine) — Duration of action: Bupivacaine 4–8 hours (longest), Lignocaine 1–2 hours, Ropivacaine 3–6 hours, Prilocaine 2–3 hours. Bupivacaine is highly protein-bound and lipophilic — hence longest acting. Cardiotoxic in overdose (treat with Intralipid).
Q4Most common complication of spinal anaesthesia isN-04
✓ bHypotension — Hypotension is the most common complication of spinal anaesthesia (incidence up to 30%), caused by sympathetic blockade → vasodilation → decreased venous return. Treatment: IV fluids, vasopressors (ephedrine, phenylephrine), lateral tilt in pregnancy. Post-dural puncture headache is common but less frequent than hypotension.
Q5Ether is NOT used in modern surgical practice because it isN-09
✓ aHighly explosive — Diethyl ether is extremely flammable and explosive, making it dangerous when combined with oxygen and electrocautery in modern ORs. Although it is an excellent anaesthetic agent (wide safety margin, good muscle relaxation), its explosivity and slow induction make it obsolete in modern practice.
Q6Local anaesthesiaS-12
✓ aBlocks transmission of nerve impulses by altering membrane permeability — LA works by blocking voltage-gated sodium channels, preventing Na⁺ influx, thus preventing action potential generation/propagation. Option b is WRONG (LA affects ALL nerve fibres, motor > sensory in higher doses). Option c is partially true but not complete. Option d is WRONG (LA does NOT work well in infected abscesses due to acidic pH).
Q7Which of the following causes dissociative anaesthesia?W-11
✓ cKetamine — Ketamine produces "dissociative anaesthesia" — a trance-like state with analgesia, amnesia, but preserved airway reflexes and spontaneous breathing. Mechanism: NMDA receptor antagonist. Used in haemodynamically unstable patients, trauma, paediatric procedures. Side effects: hallucinations (prevented by benzodiazepines), increased secretions, raised ICP.
Q8Which parameter monitoring is important for anaesthetist during major surgery?W-11, W-13
✓ dAll of the above — ASA minimum monitoring standards during anaesthesia: (1) Urine output (organ perfusion), (2) Pulse/HR (cardiac status), (3) Oxygen saturation/SpO₂ (respiratory adequacy), plus BP, EtCO₂, temperature, ECG. All are important, so "all of the above" is correct.
Q9Boyle's apparatus is forS-13
✓ aGeneral anaesthesia — Boyle's apparatus (anaesthetic machine) = the standard equipment for delivery of GENERAL ANAESTHESIA. It controls flow of O₂, N₂O, and volatile anaesthetic agents (sevoflurane, isoflurane). Named after Sir Henry Boyle who designed the first practical anaesthetic machine in 1917.

CH 19 Perioperative Management of the High-Risk Surgical Patient
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Swan-Ganz catheter measuresN-04
✓ bPulmonary capillary resistance (Pulmonary capillary wedge pressure — PCWP) — The Swan-Ganz (pulmonary artery) catheter measures: PCWP (reflects left atrial pressure / LVEDP), pulmonary artery pressure, cardiac output (thermodilution), and mixed venous O₂ saturation. PCWP is used to assess left heart function and differentiate cardiac causes of pulmonary oedema.
CH 20 Nutrition and Fluid Therapy
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Main cation of extracellular compartment isN-03
✓ aSodium (Na⁺) — Na⁺ is the predominant EXTRACELLULAR cation (135–145 mEq/L). It determines ECF osmolality and volume. Potassium (K⁺) is the main INTRACELLULAR cation (150 mEq/L inside cells). Chloride is the main extracellular anion. Memory: Na = Outside, K = Inside cells.
Q2Parenteral nutrition is best given byM-04
✓ cSubclavian vein — TPN (Total Parenteral Nutrition) must be administered via a CENTRAL vein (subclavian or internal jugular → superior vena cava) because hypertonic solutions (high osmolality ~2000 mOsm/L) would cause thrombophlebitis in peripheral veins. Subclavian is the preferred site due to lower infection risk than femoral and easier access than jugular.
Q3Contraindications for enteral feeding areN-08
✓ dNone of the above — Dysphagia for solids, inflammatory bowel disease, and distal low-output enterocutaneous fistulas are NOT contraindications to enteral feeding. True contraindications to enteral feeding: complete intestinal obstruction, paralytic ileus, high-output proximal fistula, severe haemodynamic instability. Enteral nutrition is always preferred over parenteral when the gut is functional ("If the gut works, use it").
Q4Best vein for administering Total Parenteral Nutrition isN-09
✓ aSubclavian vein — Consistent with Q2. TPN → central venous access → subclavian or internal jugular → tip in SVC. PICC lines (peripherally inserted central catheters) via basilic/cephalic vein are also used for long-term TPN.
Q5Insensible daily water loss isM-10, S-12
✓ b800–1,000 mL — Daily insensible water loss in adults: Skin (sweat/evaporation) ≈ 500 mL, Respiratory tract (expired air) ≈ 300–400 mL. Total ≈ 800–1,000 mL/day. Total daily fluid requirement: insensible loss (800–1000 mL) + urine (1500 mL) + faecal (100–200 mL) = ~2500 mL/day.
