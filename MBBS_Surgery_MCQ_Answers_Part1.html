CH 02 Shock and Blood Transfusion
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Complications of massive transfusion include all EXCEPTM-03, W-16
✓ dHypercalcemia — Massive transfusion causes HYPOcalcemia (citrate binds calcium), hyperkalaemia, acidosis, hypothermia, and coagulopathy. Hypercalcemia does NOT occur.
Q2Most suitable fluid for initial resuscitation in hypovolemic shock isM-03
✓ aRinger's Lactate (Hartmann's solution) — It is the preferred crystalloid for volume resuscitation in hemorrhagic/hypovolemic shock. It is isotonic and most closely resembles plasma.
Q3Features of Hypovolemic shock are all EXCEPTN-04
✓ bBradycardia — Hypovolemic shock classically causes TACHYCARDIA (compensatory), not bradycardia. Other features: oliguria, low BP, acidosis are all present.
Q4Weighing of swabs post-operatively is a method to assessN-08
✓ cBlood loss — Gravimetric method (weighing surgical swabs) helps estimate intraoperative and postoperative blood loss accurately.
Q5CVP measurement is useful in distinguishing betweenN-08
✓ bCardiogenic and hypovolemic shock — CVP is HIGH in cardiogenic shock (pump failure) and LOW in hypovolemic shock, making it ideal for distinguishing these two.
Q6Secondary haemorrhage occurs at the following time in relation to surgeryN-09
✓ c7–14 days following surgery — Secondary haemorrhage is due to infection/sepsis eroding vessel walls. It typically occurs 7–14 days post-injury/surgery.
Q7Secondary haemorrhage usually occurs at the following duration in relation to surgeryN-10
✓ d7 to 14 days after surgery — Confirms the above. Infection causes sloughing of vessel wall → delayed bleeding.
Q8Preoperative transfusions are considered EXCEPTW-11
✓ dHaemoglobin levels are 10 g/dL — A Hb of 10 g/dL does NOT usually require pre-op transfusion. Transfusion is indicated if Hb <8 g/dL, in major surgery, or rare blood group patients.
Q9Reactionary haemorrhage occurs withinS-12
✓ c<24 hours — Reactionary haemorrhage occurs within 24 hours of surgery/injury (usually within a few hours). It results from slipped ligatures or rise in blood pressure.
Q10The skin is warm and dry inS-13
✓ aSeptic shock (early/warm phase) — In early septic shock (distributive), peripheral vasodilation causes warm, flushed, dry skin. Haemorrhagic/hypovolemic shock = cold, clammy skin. (Note: Book answer shows "b) Burn shock" but clinically septic shock is the correct answer for warm dry skin.)
Q11External bleeding in an injured patient is best controlled byS-13
✓ aDirect pressure — Direct firm pressure over the wound is the first-line, safest, and most effective method to control external haemorrhage.
Q12Which of the following is NOT an example of distributive shock?W-13
✓ cMassive Pulmonary Embolus — Massive PE causes OBSTRUCTIVE shock, not distributive. Distributive shock = anaphylaxis, sepsis, neurogenic (spinal cord injury).
Q13Decompensation of circulatory system occurs when loss of circulating volume is more thanW-13
✓ b30–40% — Class III hemorrhage (30–40% volume loss) leads to decompensation with significant hypotension, tachycardia, and altered mental status requiring urgent blood transfusion.
Q14All of the following are major complications of massive blood transfusion EXCEPTS-15
✓ aHypokalemia — Massive transfusion causes HYPERkalemia (stored blood releases K⁺). Hypothermia, hypocalcemia, and hypomagnesaemia all occur.
Q15In case of acute trauma, best guideline for quick replacement of fluid isS-15
✓ cUrine output — Urine output (target ≥0.5 mL/kg/hr in adults) is the best practical bedside indicator of adequate fluid resuscitation and organ perfusion.
Q16Cryoprecipitate is rich source ofW-15
✓ bFactor VIII — Cryoprecipitate contains Factor VIII, von Willebrand factor, fibrinogen (Factor I), Factor XIII, and fibronectin. Key use: haemophilia A, von Willebrand disease, DIC.
Q17Shock is best assessed byW-15
✓ aUrinary output — Urine output is the best indicator of end-organ perfusion and adequacy of resuscitation. Target: ≥0.5 mL/kg/hr.
Q18Secondary haemorrhage usually occursW-16
✓ c7–14 days after injury — Due to infection/sepsis eroding vessel walls (consistent with Q6 and Q7).
Q19The best way to control external hemorrhage isS-17
✓ bExternal compression (direct pressure) — As per ATLS, firm direct pressure is the first and best method to control external hemorrhage.
Q20Complications from Massive blood transfusion include all EXCEPTS-17
✓ aHypokalemia — Same principle as Q14. Massive transfusion causes HYPERkalemia, not hypokalemia.
Q21Shock is clinically best assessed byS-17
✓ aUrine output — Consistent with Q17. Best clinical marker of perfusion status.
Q22Haemaccel containsS-17
✓ bPolygelin — Haemaccel is a gelatin-based plasma expander containing polygelin (degraded gelatin cross-linked with urea bridges) with calcium and sodium chloride.
Q23Patient with trauma who lost 30–40% of blood volume belongs to which class of haemorrhagic shock?S-18
✓ bClass III — ATLS Classification: Class I <15%, Class II 15–30%, Class III 30–40%, Class IV >40%. Class III = significant tachycardia, hypotension, altered consciousness.
Q24Adequacy of fluid replacement in a patient can be best judged byS-18
✓ cIncreased urine output — Target ≥0.5 mL/kg/hr (adult), ≥1 mL/kg/hr (child). Best indicator of renal perfusion and overall fluid adequacy.
Q25In obstructive shockS-19
✓ aReduction of preload — Obstructive shock (PE, cardiac tamponade, tension pneumothorax) causes mechanical obstruction to ventricular filling, reducing preload (PCWP/CVP may be paradoxically elevated due to obstruction, but effective preload to LV is reduced).

CH 03 Wounds, Tissue Repair and Scars
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Lacerated wound looks like an incised wound inM-04
✓ aScrotum — The scrotal skin is very thin with minimal subcutaneous fat. When lacerated, the edges appose neatly, mimicking a clean incised wound.
Q2Delayed wound healing is seen in all EXCEPTN-09
✓ cHypertension — Hypertension alone does NOT delay wound healing. Causes of delayed healing: diabetes, malignancy, infection, malnutrition, corticosteroids, anaemia, etc.
Q3Three phases of normal wound healing areM-11
✓ aInflammatory → Proliferative → Remodelling — Classic 3-phase wound healing: (1) Inflammatory/haemostasis phase (0–3 days), (2) Proliferative/granulation phase (3–21 days), (3) Remodelling/maturation phase (21 days–2 years).
Q4Southampton asepsis scoring system is forM-11
✓ bSeverity of wound infection — Southampton scoring assesses the degree/severity of surgical wound infection (grades 0–5 based on discharge, erythema, wound disruption etc.).
Q5Plaster of Paris isW-11
✓ aHemi-hydrated calcium sulphate [CaSO₄·½H₂O] — When water is added, it hydrates fully (CaSO₄·2H₂O) and sets hard. Used in orthopaedic casts and dental molds.
Q6Commonest site for pressure sore isW-12
✓ aIschium — In sitting patients, ischial tuberosities bear maximum pressure. (Sacrum is most common in bedridden/supine patients; ischium in wheelchair-bound patients. Book answer: ischium.)
Q7A clean incised wound heals byS-13
✓ bPrimary intention — Clean, apposed surgical wounds heal by primary (first) intention: minimal scar, rapid healing, no granulation tissue formation.
Q8Decubitus ulcers areS-13
✓ aPressure sores — "Decubitus" = lying down (Latin). Decubitus ulcers = pressure ulcers caused by prolonged pressure on bony prominences in immobile patients.
Q9Wound healing is worst atS-13
✓ cSternum — Sternal wounds are notoriously difficult to heal due to: constant movement with respiration, poor blood supply to skin, tension on wound edges. Legs and face heal poorly too but sternum is worst.
Q10Which does NOT fit in the description of a tidy wound?W-13
✓ dOften associated with tissue loss — A tidy wound is incised, clean, has healthy tissue edges, and has NO tissue loss. Tissue loss makes it "untidy." Untidy wounds require debridement before closure.
Q11The best period for primary wound closure of uncontaminated wound is up toW-13
✓ a6 hours — The "golden period" for primary closure of uncontaminated wounds is 6 hours (before significant bacterial colonization). Beyond this, secondary or delayed primary closure is preferred.
Q12Following are required for wound healing EXCEPTS-15
✓ dCalcium — Calcium is NOT specifically required for wound healing. Required: Zinc (collagen synthesis), Copper (cross-linking), Vitamin C (hydroxylation of proline/lysine), Vitamin A, proteins, iron.
Q13Commonest site of keloid isW-15
✓ cPresternal area — The presternal region is the most common site for keloid formation, followed by the deltoid region, ear lobule, and jaw. Dark-skinned individuals are predisposed.
Q14Delayed wound healing is seen in all EXCEPTW-15
✓ bHypertension — Hypertension alone does NOT delay wound healing. Same concept as Q2.
Q15Compartment syndrome: fasciotomy should be performed if compartmental pressures areW-16
✓ d>30 mmHg — Fasciotomy is indicated when compartment pressure exceeds 30 mmHg, or within 30 mmHg of diastolic pressure (delta pressure <30 mmHg). Delay causes Volkmann's ischaemia.
Q16Keloid scar is made up ofS-17
✓ aDense collagen — Keloids are composed of dense, haphazardly arranged thick collagen bundles (predominantly Type III collagen) with excess fibroblast activity beyond wound margins.
Q17A clean incised wound heals byS-17
✓ cPrimary intention — Repeat of Q7. Clean sutured wounds = primary intention healing.
Q18Tissue suturing glue containsS-18
✓ aCyanocrylate (Cyanoacrylate) — Tissue glues (Dermabond, Histoacryl) are based on cyanoacrylate monomer which polymerizes rapidly on contact with tissue moisture, forming a strong bond.
Q19Following number surgical blade is used most commonly to incise skin in laparotomy in adult patientS-18
✓ dNo. 22 surgical blade — No. 22 (large blade) fits a No. 4 handle. Used for large skin incisions in adults (laparotomy). No. 10 and 15 are for smaller incisions; No. 11 for stab incisions.
Q20Which method is used for skin approximation?S-18
✓ dAll of the above — Skin approximation can be achieved by: skin staplers, adhesive tapes (Steri-Strips), tissue glue, and sutures. All are valid methods.
Q21Predominant type of collagen in Keloids isS-19
✓ cType III collagen — Keloids predominantly contain Type III collagen (reticular fibres, fetal-type collagen) arranged in thick, disorganized bundles. Normal dermis is mostly Type I collagen.
Q22Class III haemorrhagic shock describes blood volume lost as percentage of totalS-19
✓ d30–40% — ATLS: Class I <15%, Class II 15–30%, Class III 30–40% (decompensated, needs blood), Class IV >40% (life-threatening).

CH 04 Basic Surgical Skills and Anastomoses
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Which of the following is NOT an Oro-Gastric tube?W-11
✓ cT-Tube — T-tube is a biliary drain placed in the common bile duct after choledochotomy — NOT an oro-gastric tube. Ryle's tube, Levine's tube, and Sengstaken-Blakemore tube all pass through the mouth/nose into the GI tract.
Q2Which suture material should be used to suture a wound in a road traffic accident?W-13
✓ dNylon (Prolene/monofilament nylon) — Non-absorbable monofilament nylon is ideal for contaminated/traumatic wounds. It has low tissue reactivity, resists infection, and can be removed later. Silk and catgut have high reactivity and are unsuitable in contaminated wounds.
Q3Following is an absorbable suture materialS-16
✓ aCatgut — Catgut (plain and chromic) is a natural absorbable suture made from sheep/cattle intestine submucosa. Silk, prolene, and nylon are all NON-absorbable.
Q4Which suture material has high tissue reaction?W-16
✓ cChromic catgut — Among the options, chromic catgut has the highest tissue reaction (it's a natural biological material). PDS and polypropylene are synthetic with minimal reaction. Plain catgut also reacts but chromic more so.
Q5Non-absorbable skin sutures from the scalp are removed in _____ daysW-16
✓ b7 days — Scalp sutures: 7 days. Face: 3–5 days. Trunk/extremity: 7–10 days. Feet: 10–14 days. Scalp sutures are removed at 7 days to avoid suture marks.
Q6All the following are absorbable sutures EXCEPTS-18
✓ cPolypropylene (Prolene) — Polypropylene is NON-absorbable. Absorbable sutures: PDS (polydioxanone), Polygalactin (Vicryl), Catgut, Polyglycolic acid (Dexon).

CH 05 Surgical Infection
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1All of the following are true about tetanus EXCEPTN-03
✓ aThere is loss of consciousness — In tetanus, consciousness is PRESERVED throughout (unlike meningitis). The tetanospasmin toxin causes spastic paralysis but does NOT affect consciousness. Trismus IS the first sign; sedatives ARE used.
Q2Hospital-acquired infections are all EXCEPTM-04
✓ dStreptococcus — Common nosocomial organisms: Pseudomonas, Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA), Acinetobacter, E. coli, Klebsiella. Streptococcus is primarily community-acquired.
Q3AIDS virus belongs toM-04
✓ aRetrovirus — HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) is an RNA retrovirus (uses reverse transcriptase to convert RNA → DNA). It belongs to the family Retroviridae, genus Lentivirus.
Q4Nosocomial infection meansN-04
✓ cHospital-acquired infection — "Nosocomial" from Greek "nosokomeion" (hospital). Infection acquired in a healthcare setting ≥48 hours after admission, or within 30 days of discharge.
Q5Most important treatment of gas gangreneN-03
✓ dWide surgical debridement or amputation — Surgical debridement/amputation is the MOST IMPORTANT treatment. Gas gangrene spreads rapidly and is fatal without surgical removal of all infected tissue. Penicillin and hyperbaric oxygen are adjuncts.
Q6Hyperbaric oxygen is useful in treatment ofM-10, W-12
✓ bGas gangrene (Clostridial myonecrosis) — HBO₂ inhibits toxin production and kills anaerobic Clostridia by creating an oxygen-rich environment. Also used in chronic non-healing wounds, CO poisoning, and decompression sickness.
Q7Different types of surgical dressings areM-11
✓ dAll of the above — Surgical dressings include: bead dressings, polymeric films (transparent films), hydrocolloids, fibrous polymers, hydrogels, and foams. Modern wound dressings are classified by their composition and mechanism.
Q8Following is NOT a proper method for abscess managementW-11
✓ bAntibiotics only if not localised — Once an abscess is localised (pointing), DRAINAGE is essential — antibiotics alone are insufficient. Antibiotics may be used as adjuncts or in early cellulitis phase before localisation.
Q9Following are techniques of sterilization EXCEPTW-11
✓ bCobalt pressure steam — "Cobalt pressure steam" is NOT a recognized sterilization method. Valid methods: steam under pressure (autoclave), ethylene oxide gas, formaldehyde, gamma radiation (cobalt-60 radiation IS used but NOT "cobalt pressure steam").
Q10Commonest organism responsible for cellulitis is fromS-12, W-12
✓ bStreptococcus (Group A β-haemolytic Streptococcus pyogenes) — The most common cause of cellulitis. Staphylococcus aureus can also cause cellulitis but Streptococcus is most common.
Q11Gas gangrene can be caused byS-13
✓ aClostridium welchii (perfringens) — C. perfringens (Type A) is the most common cause (~80%) of gas gangrene/clostridial myonecrosis. Also: C. novyi, C. septicum, C. histolyticum. All produce α-toxin (lecithinase).
Q12Ideal steam sterilization of 121°C with 15 lb/inch pressure should have hold time ofS-13
✓ b15 minutes — Standard autoclave: 121°C / 15 psi / 15 minutes. Flash autoclave: 134°C / 30 psi / 3 minutes. This kills all microorganisms including bacterial endospores.
Q13Sign in a limb cellulitis that is a "sign of bad omen"W-13
✓ dCuticular (skin) necrosis — Skin necrosis in cellulitis indicates progression to necrotising fasciitis or severe deep infection — a very serious sign requiring urgent surgical exploration. Tense swelling, redness, and fever are expected features.
Q14Best timing of giving prophylactic IV antibiotics in clean surgeryW-13
✓ bAt the induction of anaesthesia — Current WHO and NICE guidelines recommend antibiotic prophylaxis within 60 minutes before incision, ideally at induction of anaesthesia to achieve peak tissue levels at time of incision.
Q15Wet gangrene is characterized by all the following EXCEPTS-16
✓ cMummification — Mummification is a feature of DRY gangrene (the tissue dries and shrivels). Wet gangrene features: pain, no clear line of demarcation, putrefaction, foul smell, tissue oedema.
Q16Burst abdomen commonly occurs onW-16
✓ d7th day — Burst abdomen (wound dehiscence/evisceration) most commonly occurs on the 7th–10th post-operative day. Preceded by sero-sanguineous discharge ("pink fluid"). Predisposing factors: infection, malnutrition, obesity, raised intra-abdominal pressure.
Q17Risus sardonicus is associated withS-17
✓ dTetanus — Risus sardonicus = "sardonic smile" caused by sustained contraction of facial muscles (masseter + zygomaticus). It is PATHOGNOMONIC of tetanus. Caused by tetanospasmin blocking inhibitory neurotransmitters.
Q18Carbuncle is treated byS-17
✓ bCruciate incision and deroofing — Carbuncle (deep multi-locular Staph infection) requires cruciate (cross-shaped) incision to adequately drain all locules + deroofing and curettage of necrotic tissue. Antibiotics are adjuncts.
Q19Commonest organism in necrotising fasciitis with toxic shock syndromeS-18
✓ bStreptococcus pyogenes (Group A Streptococcus) — GAS is the commonest cause of Type II necrotising fasciitis with toxic shock syndrome (via superantigens). Type I is polymicrobial (synergistic).
Q20Synergistic spreading gangrene of abdominal wall is otherwise known asS-19
✓ bMeleney's synergistic hospital gangrene — Meleney's gangrene = progressive bacterial synergistic gangrene of the abdominal wall, caused by microaerophilic Streptococcus + S. aureus working together. Distinct from Fournier's (scrotal/perineal).
Q21Cellulitis is most commonly caused byW-19
✓ bStreptococci (Group A β-haemolytic Streptococcus pyogenes) — Consistent with Q10. Streptococcus is the leading cause of cellulitis worldwide.
Q2230-year-old man, clean wound without laceration, TT vaccination 10 years ago. Tetanus prophylaxis advice?PG Entrance 2020
✓ aSingle dose of TT — The patient was vaccinated 10 years ago (i.e., immunity may have waned but basic course was complete). For a clean wound with previous vaccination >5 years ago → give a TT booster (single dose). TIG (immunoglobulin) is NOT needed for clean wounds.
Tetanus prophylaxis rule: Clean wounds + vaccinated >5 years = TT booster. Dirty wounds + no/unknown vaccination = TT + TIG.
