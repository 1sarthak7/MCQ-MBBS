CH 30 Burns (Continued)
MCQ — Continued
Q15Feature of deep partial thickness burns isS-17
✓ dReduced sensations — Deep partial-thickness (deep dermal) burns destroy most of the dermis including deep nerve endings → markedly REDUCED but not absent sensation (unlike full-thickness which is completely anaesthetic). Features: pale/mottled, reduced blanching, reduced pain to pinprick, blisters may be absent.
Classification: Superficial (1st) = erythema only. Superficial partial (2nd superficial) = blisters, pink, moist, painful. Deep partial (2nd deep) = pale, reduced sensation. Full thickness (3rd) = white/charred, painless, leathery.
Q16Most commonly used IV fluid in adult burns patient isW-17
✓ dRinger's Lactate — RL (Hartmann's solution) is the standard IV fluid for burn resuscitation (Parkland formula). It is isotonic, balanced, and closest to plasma composition. Normal saline in large volumes causes hyperchloraemic acidosis. Dextrose solutions do not replace protein/electrolyte losses.
Q17According to Rule of Nine, burns of the perineum areW-17
✓ a1% — The perineum/genitalia = 1% BSA in the Rule of Nines (Wallace). This makes the total 100%: Head 9% + each arm 9% (×2=18%) + anterior trunk 18% + posterior trunk 18% + each leg 18% (×2=36%) + perineum 1% = 100%.
Q18Which of the following is NOT seen in 3rd degree burns?W-17
✓ dExtremely painful — 3rd degree (full-thickness) burns DESTROY all nerve endings → completely PAINLESS/ANAESTHETIC. This is a critical clinical point — pain is ABSENT, not increased. Features that ARE seen: loss of skin appendages (hair follicles, sweat glands), no vesicles/blisters, leathery/waxy/charred/white appearance.
Q19Most common cause of death in burns isW-17
✓ dSepticaemic shock — Overall (combining early + late), SEPSIS/septicaemia is the most common cause of death in burns. Early (<48h): hypovolaemic shock. Late (>48h): sepsis from wound infection (Pseudomonas, MRSA, fungi). Since sepsis kills more patients overall, it is cited as the leading cause of death in burns.
Q20Deep skin burns are treated withS-18
✓ aSplit thickness skin grafts — Deep burns (deep partial and full thickness) requiring skin grafting are covered with SPLIT-THICKNESS skin grafts (Thiersch grafts), not full-thickness grafts. STSG harvest a large area, can be meshed to expand coverage, and leave a donor site that heals spontaneously.
Q21Parkland's formula for fluids in burn management isS-19
✓ dTBSA% burns × wt in kg × 4 — Parkland formula: 4 mL × weight (kg) × %TBSA burned = total Ringer's Lactate in first 24 hours. Half in first 8 hours (from time of burn), half in next 16 hours. This is the most important burns formula to memorize.
CH 31 Plastic and Reconstructive Surgery
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Graft taken from another individual of the same species isM-03
✓ bAllograft (Homograft) — Graft terminology: Autograft = same individual (own tissue), Isograft = genetically identical individual (identical twin), Allograft/Homograft = same species different individual, Xenograft/Heterograft = different species (e.g., porcine skin). Allografts are temporary biological dressings for burns.
Q2Partial thickness graft is NOT taken up on followingN-03
✓ aExposed bone (without periosteum) — Split-thickness skin grafts will NOT survive on bare bone (without periosteum), bare cartilage (without perichondrium), bare tendon (without paratenon), and irradiated tissue. They DO take on muscle, granulation tissue, fascia, fat, and periosteum-covered bone.
Q3Wolfe's graft is a type of skin graft which isN-10
✓ cFull thickness (graft) — Wolfe-Krause graft = FULL THICKNESS skin graft (FTSG): includes epidermis + entire dermis. Gives better cosmetic result, less contracture, used on face/hands. Donor site must be closed directly (limited donor area). Named after John Wolfe & Fedor Krause.
Q4Who said "Skin is the best dressing"?N-10, W-14
✓ aJoseph Lister — Joseph Lister (pioneer of antiseptic surgery) coined the phrase "Skin is the best dressing." This underlies the principle of early skin cover for wounds/burns. The book also lists this as Q9 with "Skin" as the answer — both refer to the same concept.
Q5Within 48 hours of skin grafting, the graft survives due toN-10
✓ bPlasma inhibition (imbibition) — In the first 24–48 hours, the skin graft survives by PLASMATIC IMBIBITION — passive absorption of nutrients (plasma, tissue fluid) from the wound bed by capillary action/osmosis. Neovascularization (inosculation and capillary ingrowth) begins at 48–72 hours and establishes blood supply by day 5–7.
Q6Split skin graft consists ofW-11
✓ bEpidermis and variable amount of dermis — Split-thickness skin graft (STSG/Thiersch graft) = epidermis + a VARIABLE portion of dermis (thin, intermediate, or thick depending on depth harvested). Full-thickness = epidermis + entire dermis. Epidermis alone = not a practical graft.
Q7Split skin graft can be applied overS-13, S-17
✓ aMuscle — STSG takes well on: muscle (with fascia), granulation tissue, fat, periosteum, perichondrium. STSG does NOT take on: bare bone (no periosteum), bare cartilage (no perichondrium), bare tendon (no paratenon), irradiated tissue, infected wounds.
Q8Wolf's graft isS-13, S-17
✓ bFull thickness graft — Repeat of Q3. Wolfe graft = full thickness skin graft. Used for: face, eyelids, hands, fingers, areas needing minimal contracture and good cosmesis.
Q9........... is the best dressingW-13
✓ bSkin — "Skin is the best dressing" — Joseph Lister's principle. The body's own skin provides the ideal barrier: controls evaporative loss, prevents infection, provides mechanical protection, and promotes healing. This underpins the importance of early wound cover.
Q10Abbé-Estlander flap is used in the reconstruction ofW-14
✓ bLip — The Abbé-Estlander flap is a full-thickness lip-switch flap used for LIP reconstruction. It transfers tissue from one lip to the other (upper to lower or vice versa) based on a labial artery pedicle. Used when >1/3 of lip is lost due to trauma or tumour resection.
Q11Split skin graft for first 48 hrs survives onS-16
✓ dPlasma imbibition — Repeat of Q5. First 24–48 hours = plasmatic imbibition (passive diffusion of nutrients). Day 2–5 = inosculation (alignment of graft and host capillaries). Day 5 onwards = true neovascularisation/revascularisation establishes blood flow.

CH 32 Disaster Surgery
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Hyperbaric oxygen is useful in treatment ofM-10, W-12
✓ bGas gangrene — HBO₂ kills anaerobic Clostridia by creating a hyperoxic environment, inhibits toxin production, and promotes phagocytosis. Also used in: CO poisoning, decompression sickness, chronic non-healing wounds, frostbite (adjunct). This is a repeat question from Ch. 5.
Q2In triage, Red colour label indicates the case requiringM-11
✓ cImmediate medical help — MASS CASUALTY TRIAGE colour coding: RED = Immediate (life-threatening but salvageable — highest priority), YELLOW = Delayed (serious but stable), GREEN = Minor (walking wounded), BLACK = Expectant/Dead (unsurvivable or already dead). Red tag = treat FIRST.
Mnemonic: "RED = Rapid, treat immediately. BLACK = Beyond help."
Q3Gas gangrene can be caused byS-13
✓ aClostridium welchii (perfringens) — C. perfringens Type A is the most common cause (~80%). Other species: C. novyi, C. septicum, C. histolyticum, C. sordellii. All are anaerobic gram-positive spore-forming bacilli. "All of above" (option d) is also shown in the book — but the primary/most important organism is C. perfringens/welchii.
Q4Alkalization of urine is indicated inW-13
✓ bCrush syndrome — Crush syndrome (traumatic rhabdomyolysis) → massive release of myoglobin → myoglobinuria → acute renal failure. Alkalising the urine (with IV sodium bicarbonate) prevents precipitation of myoglobin casts in renal tubules (myoglobin is more soluble in alkaline urine), protecting kidneys. Also: aggressive IV fluid, forced diuresis.
Q5Blast pressure wave stress injuries to organs EXCEPTW-13
✓ dBone — Primary blast injury (from overpressure wave) damages AIR-CONTAINING organs preferentially: ear (tympanic membrane rupture — most sensitive), lungs (pulmonary barotrauma/blast lung), GIT (bowel perforation). SOLID organs like bone are relatively protected from primary blast wave (they are injured by secondary/tertiary blast mechanisms).
Q6Commonest organ injured in blast injury isW-16
✓ bLung — The LUNG is the most commonly injured organ in blast injury (blast lung syndrome = bilateral pulmonary contusion + haemorrhage). The ear (tympanic membrane) is the most SENSITIVE organ, but lung injury is the most COMMON organ injury in blast casualties.
Q7'Limb salvage' primarily depends onS-17
✓ aVascular injury — Vascular integrity is the PRIMARY determinant of limb salvage in trauma. Even with severe bone, nerve, or skin injuries, a limb can potentially be salvaged if vascular supply is restored. Prolonged ischaemia (>6 hours) leads to irreversible muscle death → amputation. Vascular repair takes priority in mangled extremity.
Q8All prone for injury in explosive blast wave EXCEPTW-17
✓ cLiver — Liver is a solid organ and relatively resistant to PRIMARY blast injury. Organs most vulnerable to blast overpressure: tympanic membrane (most sensitive), lungs, GIT (hollow organs), and sinuses. Secondary blast (fragments) and tertiary blast (being thrown) cause injury to all organs including solid ones.
CH 33 History Taking & Clinical Examination — Musculoskeletal
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Winging of scapula indicates injury to which nerve?S-14
✓ bLong thoracic nerve (of Bell) — The long thoracic nerve (C5,6,7) innervates the serratus anterior muscle, which holds the scapula against the chest wall. Injury (e.g., axillary surgery, chest wall injury) → serratus anterior paralysis → WINGING of scapula (medial border of scapula protrudes when pushing against wall).
Q2In tennis elbow the pain is localised nearS-14
✓ bThe lateral epicondyle of humerus — Tennis elbow (lateral epicondylitis) = enthesopathy at the LATERAL epicondyle (common extensor origin, particularly ECRB). Pain on resisted wrist extension, gripping. Golfer's elbow = MEDIAL epicondyle (common flexor origin). "Tennis = Lateral" — both start with consonants.
Q3Allen test assessesW-14
✓ cIntegrity of palmar arch — Allen's test assesses the patency of the radial and ulnar arteries and their contribution to the PALMAR ARCH (superficial and deep). Both arteries compressed → hand blanches → release one → hand flushes if that artery contributes adequately. Essential before radial artery cannulation/harvest to ensure ulnar artery can perfuse the hand alone.

CH 36 Upper Limb — Pathology, Assessment and Management
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Treatment of hand infections includes EXCEPTW-11
✓ aDelayed mobilization — Delayed mobilization is WRONG in hand infections — it leads to stiffness, adhesions, and poor functional outcome. EARLY mobilization (as soon as infection controlled) is essential to prevent hand stiffness. Treatment principles: incision and drainage, antibiotics, elevation, and EARLY MOBILIZATION.
Q2Felon is the accumulation of pusW-13
✓ bIn pulp space — Felon = infection/abscess of the FINGERTIP PULP SPACE (distal closed space). Very painful due to tight fibrous septa. Can cause pressure necrosis of distal phalanx. Paronychia = beside nail fold. Tenosynovitis = tendon sheath. Web space abscess = web space.
Q3Dupuytren's contracture is associated with all EXCEPTS-14
✓ bDiabetics — Dupuytren's contracture (fibroproliferative disease of palmar fascia → ring & little finger flexion) is associated with: Cirrhosis/alcoholism, Epileptics on phenytoin (sodium hydantoin), Peyronie's disease (penile fibromatosis), trauma, family history, Northern European males. Diabetes is NOT a classic association (unlike Dupuytren's contracture of the foot/Ledderhose disease).

CH 40 Infection of Bones and Joints
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1The commonest ulcer to become a malignant (Marjolin's) ulcer isS-14
✓ bVenous ulcer — Marjolin's ulcer = malignant transformation (usually squamous cell carcinoma) occurring in a CHRONIC SCAR or chronic ulcer. The most common precursor is a VENOUS (varicose/stasis) ulcer, followed by burn scars, osteomyelitic sinuses, and pressure sores. The transformation is insidious and painless (due to scar anaesthesia).
CH 42 Skin and Subcutaneous Tissue
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions (57 Questions)
Q1Strawberry angioma in a child is best treated byM-03
✓ aMasterly inactivity (watchful waiting) — Strawberry haemangioma (infantile haemangioma) appears at 1–4 weeks, grows for ~6–9 months, then SPONTANEOUSLY INVOLUTES in >90% of cases by age 7–9 years. Treatment: observe first. Intervention only if: amblyopia (periorbital), airway compromise, ulceration, or failure to involute. Propranolol is now first-line when treatment IS needed.
Q2Which malignancy is associated with immunosuppression?M-03
✓ aKaposi's sarcoma — KS (caused by HHV-8) classically occurs in immunocompromised patients: AIDS, transplant recipients on immunosuppression. It is an AIDS-defining illness. Appears as painless purplish skin nodules/patches. Epidemic KS (AIDS-related) is the most aggressive form.
Q3Ingrowing toe nail is a type ofM-03
✓ dDegenerative process — Ingrowing toenail (onychocryptosis) is classified as a degenerative condition (not congenital, neoplastic, or foreign body). It is caused by improper nail cutting, tight footwear, or nail deformity, leading to nail edge penetrating surrounding soft tissue with inflammatory reaction.
Q4Plexiform neurofibromatosis affects more commonlyM-03
✓ dPeripheral nerve — Repeat from Ch. 9. Plexiform neurofibromas involve peripheral nerve plexuses diffusely ("bag of worms"). Pathognomonic of NF-1 (von Recklinghausen's disease).
Q5The edge of a basal cell carcinoma isM-03
✓ dNone of the above (i.e., Rolled/Pearly edge) — BCC has a characteristic ROLLED, PEARLY/BEADED edge (everted rolled edge with telangiectasia). NOT sloping (healing ulcer), NOT everted (carcinoma/SCC/Marjolin's), NOT undermined (tuberculous/rodent ulcer edge is actually rolled). The correct term not listed as an option is "rolled" or "pearly rolled."
Q6Plexiform neurofibromatosis commonly affects following cranial nerveN-03
✓ b5th Trigeminal — Repeat from Ch. 9. Among cranial nerves, CN V (trigeminal) is most commonly affected by plexiform neurofibroma in NF-1, causing facial disfigurement.
Q7Sebaceous cyst CANNOT occur inN-03, S-12
✓ dSoles and palms — Sebaceous cysts (epidermoid/pilar cysts) arise from sebaceous glands or blocked hair follicles. They CANNOT occur on soles and palms because these areas have NO HAIR FOLLICLES and NO SEBACEOUS GLANDS. Sebaceous cysts commonly occur on: scalp, face, neck, back, scrotum, behind ears.
Q8Ulcer is defined asN-03
✓ dBreach in continuity of epithelium — An ulcer is a CIRCUMSCRIBED BREACH/LOSS of an epithelial surface (skin or mucosa) that fails to heal by primary intention. Key: breach in epithelial continuity with loss of substance. A wound = breach in surface. A sinus = blind-ending tract. A fistula = abnormal connection between two epithelial surfaces.
Q9Treatment of primary malignant melanoma of skin isN-03
✓ dWide excision — Primary treatment of cutaneous melanoma is WIDE LOCAL EXCISION with adequate margins (based on Breslow thickness): <1mm → 1cm margin, 1–2mm → 1–2cm, >2mm → 2cm. Radiotherapy and chemotherapy have limited roles. Immunotherapy (pembrolizumab, ipilimumab) is for metastatic disease.
Q10Increased exposure to ultraviolet light causesN-03
✓ aBasal cell carcinoma — UV radiation (UVB 290–320 nm primarily) is the most important risk factor for BCC. BCC is the most common skin cancer and most common cancer overall in fair-skinned individuals. UV causes p53 mutations and patched gene (PTCH1) mutations. SCC is also UV-related but BCC is most strongly linked.
Q11Glomus tumour isM-04
✓ bAngiomyoneuroma — Glomus tumour = benign tumour of the glomus body (neuromyoarterial apparatus — a specialized AV anastomosis for temperature regulation). Histologically = angiomyoneuroma (vascular + smooth muscle + nerve elements). Classic presentation: SUBUNGUAL (under fingernail), EXQUISITELY PAINFUL, point tenderness, cold sensitivity. Tiny (few mm), treated by excision.
Q12The baby is normal at birth in which type of haemangioma?M-04
✓ aStrawberry haemangioma — Strawberry haemangioma (infantile haemangioma) is NOT present at birth — it appears at 1–4 weeks (baby is NORMAL at birth), grows rapidly for 6–12 months, then involutes. Port-wine stain, salmon patch, venous angioma are ALL present at birth.
Q13Commonest soft tissue tumour isM-04, S-13
✓ aLipoma — Lipoma is the most common benign soft tissue tumour overall, arising from mature adipocytes. It is the most common subcutaneous soft tissue tumour. Soft, lobulated, mobile, non-tender, compressible. Sites: back, neck, shoulder, abdominal wall. Multiple lipomas = Dercum's disease (painful), Madelung's disease (multiple symmetric).
Q14Carcinoma which develops in a scar isM-04
✓ bMarjolin's ulcer — Marjolin's ulcer = malignant transformation (usually SCC) arising in a chronic scar, chronic ulcer, or burn scar. Features: painless (scar is anaesthetic), no regional lymph node metastasis (scar has no lymphatics initially), late presentation, aggressive behaviour when metastasis does occur. Named after Jean-Nicolas Marjolin.
Q15Commonest soft tissue tumour in AIDS isN-04
✓ bLymphoma — Non-Hodgkin's lymphoma is the commonest soft tissue/systemic tumour in AIDS. (Kaposi's sarcoma is the commonest skin tumour in AIDS, but lymphoma predominates among soft tissue tumours.)
Q16Lymphatic spread is NOT seen inN-04
✓ dBasal cell carcinoma — BCC (Rodent ulcer) is locally invasive but ALMOST NEVER metastasises — no lymphatic or haematogenous spread in routine cases (<0.1% metastasis rate). This makes it the least aggressive of all skin malignancies despite local destruction. SCC, melanoma, and adenocarcinoma all spread via lymphatics.
Q17Tear ulcer is also called asN-04
✓ dRodent ulcer — Rodent ulcer = BCC (basal cell carcinoma). Called "rodent" because it slowly gnaws/erodes surrounding tissue like a rodent. The EDGE is characteristically rolled and pearly. Commonest site: face (around nose, inner canthus, nasolabial fold). "Tear ulcer" = Rodent ulcer = BCC.
Q18Drug of choice in isolated limb perfusion in melanomaN-04
✓ aMelphalan — Isolated limb perfusion (ILP) with hyperthermic Melphalan is the gold standard for in-transit melanoma of the limb. Melphalan (an alkylating agent) is perfused at high concentration directly into the limb (isolated from systemic circulation) with hyperthermia (39–41°C), achieving very high tumour drug concentrations with minimal systemic toxicity.
Q19Carbuncle is the name given toN-08
✓ dInfective gangrene of subcutaneous tissue — Carbuncle = deep, multi-locular Staphylococcal infection involving hair follicles + surrounding subcutaneous tissue = infective gangrene of subcutaneous tissue with multiple discharging sinuses. Larger and deeper than furuncle (boil). Common sites: nape of neck, upper back. Treatment: cruciate incision + deroofing + antibiotics.
Q20Kaposi's sarcoma is a frequent manifestation inN-08
✓ dAIDS — Kaposi's sarcoma (caused by HHV-8) is an AIDS-defining illness and the most common malignancy in HIV/AIDS. Presents as painless, purplish, multifocal skin lesions + visceral involvement. Epidemic (AIDS-related) KS responds to HAART.
Q21Ultraviolet light is a carcinogen affectingN-08
✓ bSkin — UV radiation (UVA + UVB) is the primary carcinogen for SKIN cancers: BCC, SCC, and melanoma. UVB causes direct DNA damage (pyrimidine dimers, p53 mutations). UVA causes indirect oxidative damage. Skin is the target organ for UV carcinogenesis.
Q22Absence of pain in ulcer is suggestive ofN-08
✓ dNeuropathic ulcer — Painless ulcer = NEUROPATHIC (sensory loss). Diabetic foot ulcers, leprosy, tabes dorsalis, syringomyelia. Arterial ulcers = painful. Venous ulcers = mild ache/heaviness. Marjolin's ulcer = painless (scar anaesthesia). The most classic painless ulcer = neuropathic/trophic ulcer.
Q23A boil is due to staphylococcal infection of theN-09
✓ aHair follicle — A boil (furuncle) = acute staphylococcal infection of a hair follicle and its surrounding tissue. Begins as folliculitis → progresses to a tender, inflamed, pus-filled nodule → spontaneous discharge. Carbuncle = multiple coalescing furuncles. Hydradenitis suppurativa = sweat gland infection.
Q24Which lipomas are likely to undergo malignant change?N-09
✓ dRetroperitoneal lipoma — Deep-seated lipomas (retroperitoneal, intramuscular) are more likely to undergo malignant transformation to liposarcoma than superficial (subcutaneous) ones. Retroperitoneal liposarcoma is the most common retroperitoneal sarcoma. Any rapidly enlarging, deep, fixed lipoma should be excised and histologically examined.
Q25Commonest malignancy in HIV patient isM-10
✓ aKaposi's sarcoma — KS is the most common malignancy in HIV/AIDS. It is an AIDS-defining illness caused by HHV-8. Common in MSM with HIV. Presents with violaceous skin/mucosal lesions. HAART reduces incidence dramatically.
Q26Commonest organism responsible for cellulitisM-10, S-12, W-12
✓ bStreptococcus pyogenes (Group A beta-haemolytic Streptococcus) — Repeat. Streptococcus is the most common cause of cellulitis worldwide. Treatment: penicillin/amoxicillin (or cephalexin). MRSA cellulitis is increasingly common (treat with clindamycin/TMP-SMX/doxycycline).
Q27Sebaceous cyst is described as aN-10
✓ aDistension cyst — Sebaceous (epidermoid) cysts are RETENTION/DISTENSION cysts — formed by blockage of a sebaceous gland duct or hair follicle infundibulum → keratin/sebum accumulates → cyst distends. Features: punctum (blocked duct opening), cheesy contents (keratin), horn projecting from punctum.
Q28Hutchinson's freckle is a type ofN-10
✓ bMelanoma (Lentigo maligna) — Hutchinson's freckle = Lentigo maligna = melanoma in situ occurring on chronically sun-damaged skin of elderly patients (face, neck). Large, flat, irregularly pigmented patch. Hutchinson's melanotic freckle → if invasive component develops = Lentigo maligna melanoma. Very slow-growing. Named after Sir Jonathan Hutchinson.
Q29The most lethal malignancy of the skin isM-11
✓ bMelanoma — Malignant melanoma has the WORST prognosis of all skin cancers, despite representing only ~5% of skin malignancies. It accounts for ~75% of skin cancer deaths due to early, aggressive metastasis (lymphatic + haematogenous). BCC is most common but least lethal. SCC is intermediate.
Q30Features of hypertrophied scar EXCEPTW-11
✓ bInfection promoting keloid formation — Infection promotes HYPERTROPHIC SCAR, NOT keloid formation specifically. Features of hypertrophic scar: remains WITHIN wound boundaries (unlike keloid which extends beyond), occurs in children, on flexor surfaces, after infection, and REGRESSES spontaneously (unlike keloid).
Q31Most common site of hypertrophic scar isS-12
✓ cPresternal area — Hypertrophic scars AND keloids are both most common in the PRESTERNAL region, followed by deltoid, ear lobule. Note: presternal area is also the most common site for KELOID. The distinction: hypertrophic scar remains within wound, keloid extends beyond.
Q32Cock's peculiar tumour isS-12
✓ aAn infected sebaceous cyst — Cock's peculiar tumour = infected/ulcerated sebaceous cyst of the SCALP that mimics a malignant tumour (fungating, foul-smelling, ulcerated appearance on the scalp). Named after Edward Cock. It is entirely BENIGN — treatment is excision after controlling infection.
Q33Total Hip Replacement was described byS-12
✓ bJohn Charnley — Sir John Charnley (1961) pioneered total hip arthroplasty using a metal-on-polyethylene low-friction arthroplasty (LFA) — small femoral head + UHMWPE acetabular cup + bone cement. Called "the father of modern THR." Hugh Owen Thomas = Thomas splint. Ilizarov = circular external fixator. Robert Jones = Jones bandage.
Q34All are brilliantly transilluminant swellings EXCEPTW-12
✓ bDermoid cyst — Dermoid cysts are NOT brilliantly transilluminant because they contain SOLID elements (hair, teeth, sebum, epithelial debris) in addition to fluid → opaque. Brilliantly transilluminant swellings (thin-walled, fluid-only): Cystic hygroma (lymphangioma), Meningocoele (CSF), Cyst of epididymis. Hydrocele also transilluminates.
Q35Universal tumour refers toS-13
✓ cLipoma — Lipoma is called the "universal tumour" because it can occur ANYWHERE in the body where adipose tissue is found — subcutaneous, subfascial, intramuscular, retroperitoneal, mediastinal, intracranial, intraspinal. Most common benign soft tissue tumour everywhere.
Q36Hydradenitis suppurativa: which is true?W-13
✓ bAbscesses and sinus tract of axillary skin — Hidradenitis suppurativa (HS) = chronic suppurative disease of apocrine sweat gland-bearing areas (axilla, groin, perianal, inframammary). Features: recurrent abscesses → sinuses → scarring → keloid. NOT to be confused with suppurative lymphadenitis (which is a lymph node infection).
Q37Commonest tumour of the subcutaneous tissue isS-14
✓ bLipoma — Repeat of Q13 and Q35. Lipoma = most common subcutaneous tumour. Soft, fluctuant, mobile, lobulated, non-tender, brilliantly transilluminant (thinly walled lipomas).
Q38The origin of malignant melanoma isS-14
✓ aEctoderm — Melanocytes are derived from NEURAL CREST cells (which originate from ECTODERM). Neural crest cells migrate to skin, uveal tract, meninges, mucosal surfaces. Hence melanoma can arise from skin (most common), eye (uveal melanoma), mucosal surfaces, and rarely meninges.
Q39Most common type of ulcer edge seen in tuberculosis isS-14
✓ aUndermined (Undetermined in book = Undermined) — Tuberculous ulcer edge = UNDERMINED (overhanging edges with bluish-grey/lilac discolouration). The edge is thin, bluish, and undercut — skin hangs over the ulcer base. Other edges: Punched-out = gummatous/syphilitic/ischaemic, Sloping = healing, Everted = carcinoma, Rolled = BCC.
Q40Metastasis is NOT common with which tumour?S-14
✓ cBasal cell carcinoma — BCC virtually NEVER metastasises (<0.1%). It is locally aggressive but non-metastatic. SCC metastasises in ~5%. Melanoma is highly metastatic. BCC is the "malignant tumour that does not kill" (unless neglected for decades in very rare cases).
Q41All the following is true about keloid EXCEPTS-14
✓ bIt does NOT extend beyond the original wound — This is FALSE about keloid. KELOID DOES extend BEYOND the original wound boundaries (unlike hypertrophic scar which stays within). Keloid features: extends beyond wound, does NOT regress, vascular, tender, itchy, recurs after excision. Type III collagen predominates.
Q42Chronic burrowing ulcer is caused byW-14
✓ aMicroaerophilic streptococci (Meleney's ulcer) — Meleney's chronic burrowing ulcer (progressive synergistic gangrene) is caused by MICROAEROPHILIC STREPTOCOCCI working synergistically with Staphylococcus aureus. It tunnels under skin, creating undermined, spreading necrosis. Distinct from necrotising fasciitis. Treated with wide debridement + antibiotics.
Q43Punched out ulcer edge is most commonly seen inW-14
✓ bGummatous ulcer (syphilitic) — PUNCHED-OUT edge (vertical walls, as if cut by a hole-punch) is most characteristic of: gummatous/syphilitic ulcer (tertiary syphilis). Also seen in: ischaemic/arterial ulcers, trophic/neuropathic ulcers. Tuberculosis = undermined. BCC = rolled. SCC = everted.
Q44Melanoma staging is based onW-14
✓ cBoth (Breslow + Clark's) — Melanoma thickness measurement: Breslow thickness (in mm from granular cell layer to deepest invasive cell) — most important prognostic factor. Clark's level (anatomical depth: I–V). Both are used together. Breslow is more reproducible and prognostically superior. TNM staging also incorporates sentinel lymph node status.
Q45All of the following is a regressing tumour EXCEPTW-14
✓ aPort-wine stain (Naevus flammeus) — Port-wine stain does NOT regress — it is permanent and often darkens/thickens with age. Regressing vascular lesions: Strawberry haemangioma (involutes by age 7–9), Salmon patch (most disappear by 1 year), Juvenile/capillary angioma. Port-wine stain = capillary malformation (not haemangioma) = DOES NOT INVOLUTE.
Q46Bowen's diseaseW-14
✓ dAll of the above — Bowen's disease = squamous cell carcinoma IN SITU (intraepidermal SCC). Features: mimics chronic dermatosis (eczema/psoriasis-like plaque), IS premalignant (can progress to invasive SCC), presents with pruritis, erythematous well-defined scaly plaques. Associated with HPV, arsenic, UV exposure. Treatment: 5-FU cream, PDT, excision.
Q47Type of melanoma affecting soles and palmsS-16
✓ dAcral lentiginous melanoma — ALM occurs on ACRAL (extremity) surfaces: palms, soles, and subungual (under nails). It is the most common melanoma in dark-skinned (Asian, African) populations. Often diagnosed late (mistaken for haematoma/fungal nail). Subungual ALM: Hutchinson's sign = pigment extending onto proximal nail fold.
Q48Melanoma can arise from all sites EXCEPTS-16
✓ bSpleen — Melanocytes (neural crest derived) are present in: skin (most common), uveal tract/retina (eye), mucosal surfaces, meninges (leptomeninges), inner ear. The SPLEEN has no melanocytes and cannot be a primary site for melanoma. Splenic melanoma is only from metastasis, not primary.
Q49Variety of capillary haemangioma that usually disappears before first birthdayW-16
✓ bSalmon patch (Naevus simplex/Stork bite) — Salmon patch = flat, pink/red capillary malformation on nape of neck ("stork bite"), forehead, or eyelids. Present at birth, FADES in >90% within first year. Strawberry haemangioma involutes by age 7–9 (not by 1 year). Port-wine stain never involutes.
Q50Cutaneous SCC >2cm: clearance margin during surgical excision should beW-16
✓ c1 cm — For cutaneous SCC >2 cm (high-risk), excision margin should be ≥1 cm. Low-risk SCC (<2 cm, well-differentiated) = 4–6 mm margin. High-risk features (size >2 cm, poor differentiation, depth >6 mm, perineural invasion, immunosuppression) warrant wider margins.
Q51Macroscopic variant of cutaneous melanoma with least metastatic potentialW-16
✓ cLentigo maligna melanoma — LMM has the BEST prognosis and least metastatic potential among melanoma subtypes. It grows along the surface (radial growth phase) for years before vertical invasion. Occurs in elderly on chronically sun-damaged skin. Nodular melanoma has the WORST prognosis (rapid vertical growth, no radial growth phase).
Q52Commonest site of rodent ulcer isS-17
✓ bFace — BCC (rodent ulcer) occurs predominantly on the FACE (>80%), particularly: inner canthus, nasolabial fold, alar base, periorbital region, scalp. The "H-zone" of the face (central face) is highest risk. Rare on trunk and limbs. Almost never on palms/soles.
Q53Moh's micrographic surgery is done forW-17
✓ aBasal cell carcinoma — Mohs micrographic surgery (MMS) = serial horizontal sectioning with real-time frozen section margin control → complete margin analysis → highest cure rate with maximal tissue conservation. GOLD STANDARD for: recurrent BCC, high-risk BCC (H-zone face), morphoeic BCC, SCC in high-risk sites. (S-19 answer = all of above including BCC, dermatofibrosarcoma, lentigo maligna.)
Q54Kasai's procedure is done forS-17
✓ aBiliary atresia — Kasai portoenterostomy (hepatoportoenterostomy) is the initial surgical treatment for biliary atresia, performed in infants before 60–90 days of age. A Roux-en-Y loop of jejunum is anastomosed to the porta hepatis to re-establish bile flow. If Kasai fails → liver transplantation (consistent with Q9 in Ch. 8).
Q55Cellulitis is most commonly caused byS-18
✓ bStreptococci — Repeat. Group A Streptococcus (Streptococcus pyogenes) is the most common cause of non-purulent cellulitis. Staphylococcus aureus causes purulent cellulitis/abscess.
Q56Moh's micrographic surgery used forS-19
✓ dAll of the above — MMS is used for: BCC (recurrent/high-risk), dermatofibrosarcoma protuberans (DFSP — requires wide margins, MMS reduces recurrence), lentigo maligna (for precise margin control). The technique provides 100% margin evaluation, maximizing tissue conservation.
Q57Elderly man with long-standing mole on face, increasing in size, irregular borders. Diagnosis?PG 2020
✓ aSuperficial spreading melanoma — The clinical features (long-standing mole, increasing size, irregular borders, elderly patient, face) describe SUPERFICIAL SPREADING MELANOMA (SSM) — the most common melanoma type (70%). Features: asymmetry, irregular border, colour variation, diameter >6mm. However, the combination of "elderly + face + long-standing" could also suggest lentigo maligna (book marks SSM). SSM has radial growth phase with characteristic border irregularity.

🔑 Master High-Yield Cheat Sheet — Skin & Subcutaneous

  • Rodent ulcer = BCC = Rolled pearly edge = Never metastasises = Commonest on face
  • Marjolin's ulcer = SCC in chronic scar/ulcer = Painless = Aggressive when metastasises
  • Melanoma = Most lethal skin cancer = Wide excision = Breslow + Clark's staging = SSM most common type
  • ALM = Palms/soles/subungual = Most common melanoma in dark skin
  • Lentigo maligna = Elderly + sun-damaged face = Best prognosis melanoma
  • Keloid = BEYOND wound + doesn't regress + presternal. Hypertrophic = WITHIN wound + regresses
  • Sebaceous cyst = Retention cyst = Cannot occur on palms/soles
  • Lipoma = Universal tumour = Most common soft tissue/subcutaneous tumour
  • Glomus tumour = Subungual + Exquisitely painful + Cold sensitive = Angiomyoneuroma
  • Strawberry haemangioma = Normal at birth, appears 1–4 weeks, involutes by age 7–9
  • Salmon patch = Present at birth, fades by age 1 year
  • Port-wine stain = Present at birth, DOES NOT involute = treat with pulsed dye laser
  • Bowen's disease = SCC in situ = Premalignant = mimics eczema
  • Mohs surgery = BCC + DFSP + lentigo maligna = 100% margin control
  • Dermoid cyst = NOT transilluminant (contains solid elements). Cystic hygroma = brilliantly transilluminant

🧠 Quick Rules for Ulcer Edges

  • Sloping = Healing ulcer
  • Undermined (bluish) = Tuberculous ulcer
  • Punched-out = Gummatous/syphilitic, ischaemic, neuropathic
  • Rolled/Pearly = BCC (Rodent ulcer)
  • Everted/Raised = Carcinoma (SCC, Marjolin's)
  • Painless ulcer = Neuropathic/trophic (diabetes, leprosy, tabes dorsalis)

🔬 Plastic Surgery Quick Facts

  • Wolfe graft = Full thickness graft (epidermis + entire dermis)
  • Split skin graft = Epidermis + variable dermis = Used for large areas including over muscle
  • STSG fails on: bare bone, bare tendon, bare cartilage, infected wound
  • Graft survival 0–48h = plasma imbibition. 48h–5d = inosculation. After 5d = neovascularisation
  • Allograft = same species. Autograft = same individual. Xenograft = different species
  • Abbé-Estlander flap = Lip reconstruction
