CH 20 Nutrition and Fluid Therapy (Continued)
MCQ — Continued from Part 2
Q6Fluid calculation requirement formula commonly used isM-11
✓ aParkland formula — Parkland formula for burns: 4 mL × weight (kg) × % BSA burned = total fluid (Ringer's Lactate) in first 24 hours. Half given in first 8 hrs, remaining half in next 16 hrs. Modified Brooke: 2 mL × kg × % BSA. Muir & Barclay is another formula but Parkland is most widely used.
Q7Which of the following is NOT an indication for parenteral nutrition?W-11
✓ cHernia surgery — Simple elective hernia surgery does NOT require parenteral nutrition. Patients can resume oral feeding post-operatively. TPN is indicated when the gut cannot be used: proximal intestinal fistula (GI losses), prolonged ileus, severe pancreatitis (gut rest needed), short bowel syndrome, etc.
Q8Sodium content of one litre of isotonic saline isW-15
✓ b154 mEq — Normal (0.9%) saline contains 154 mEq/L of Na⁺ and 154 mEq/L of Cl⁻. Osmolality ≈ 308 mOsm/L. Plasma Na⁺ is 135–145 mEq/L — saline is slightly hypertonic relative to plasma Na⁺ but isosmotic overall. Large volumes cause hyperchloraemic metabolic acidosis.
Q9TPN is indicated in all EXCEPTS-17
✓ cSepsis — Sepsis alone is NOT an indication for TPN. In sepsis, enteral nutrition is PREFERRED (if gut function is present) as it maintains gut mucosal integrity and prevents bacterial translocation. TPN may worsen outcomes in sepsis (hyperglycaemia, line infections). TPN is indicated in short bowel, burns (if enteral not tolerated), and high-output fistulas.
Q10Best skin disinfection lasting more than 4 hours for central line insertion isS-17
✓ cChlorhexidine — Chlorhexidine gluconate (2%) is the gold standard antiseptic for central venous catheter (CVC) site preparation. It has residual antimicrobial activity lasting 4–6+ hours due to binding to skin proteins. Superior to povidone-iodine for CVC-associated bloodstream infection prevention.
Q11Maintenance fluid requirements in neonate in mL/kg/day isS-17
✓ b120–150 mL/kg/day — Neonatal fluid requirements (Holliday-Segar): Day 1: 60–80 mL/kg/day, Day 2: 80–120 mL/kg/day, Day 3 onwards: 120–150 mL/kg/day. For children >10 kg: 100 mL/kg for first 10 kg + 50 mL/kg for 10–20 kg + 20 mL/kg for each kg above 20 kg.
Q12All the following are common complications of TPN EXCEPTW-17
✓ aHypoglycemia — TPN typically causes HYPERglycaemia (due to high glucose load), NOT hypoglycaemia. Hypoglycaemia can occur if TPN is suddenly stopped (rebound), but during TPN, hyperglycaemia is the issue. True TPN complications: fluid overload, hepatic dysfunction (steatosis, cholestasis), vitamin/micronutrient deficiency, catheter sepsis, electrolyte disturbances.
Q13All of the following are supplemented in TPN EXCEPTS-18
✓ dFibres — TPN is delivered intravenously and bypasses the gut entirely. Dietary fibre CANNOT be given parenterally. TPN provides: carbohydrates (dextrose — energy source), lipids (fat emulsions — essential fatty acids + energy), proteins (amino acid solutions), vitamins, minerals, and trace elements.
CH 21 Postoperative Care
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Commonest site for pressure sore isW-12
✓ aIschium — In wheelchair-bound/sitting patients, the ischial tuberosities bear maximum sustained pressure. The sacrum is the most common site in bedridden/supine patients. Other common sites: greater trochanter (lateral position), heel, malleolus, occiput.
Q2Decubitus ulcers areS-13
✓ aPressure sores — "Decubitus" = Latin for "lying down." Decubitus ulcers = pressure ulcers from sustained pressure causing ischaemia and tissue necrosis over bony prominences in immobile patients. Also called bedsores. Prevention: 2-hourly turning, pressure-relieving mattresses.
Q3The most pathognomonic sign of an impending wound rupture isS-15
✓ bSerosanguinous discharge — Sudden appearance of pink/salmon-coloured (serosanguinous) discharge from an abdominal wound on day 7–10 post-op is PATHOGNOMONIC of impending burst abdomen (wound dehiscence). This "pink fluid" warns the surgeon before the wound actually opens.

CH 23 Introduction to Trauma
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1First step in trauma isW-19
✓ dMaintenance of airway — ATLS primary survey: A-B-C-D-E. Airway (with cervical spine protection) is ALWAYS the first priority in trauma. A dead airway kills the patient within minutes — before bleeding, dehydration, or reconstruction can be addressed.
CH 24 Early Assessment and Management of Trauma
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1In severe injury, first to be maintained isM-10
✓ cAirway — Consistent with ATLS principles. "A" for Airway (with C-spine protection) is always first. Without a patent airway, all other interventions are futile. Clear airway → assess breathing → circulation (control haemorrhage) → disability → exposure.
Q2External bleeding in an injured patient is best controlled byS-13
✓ aDirect pressure — Firm, direct manual pressure over the wound is the most effective, safest first-line method to control external haemorrhage. Tourniquets are second-line for limb bleeding uncontrolled by pressure. Adrenaline is not used for haemorrhage control.
Q3The diagnosis of flail chest is done byW-13
✓ aClinical examination — Flail chest is a CLINICAL diagnosis. Paradoxical movement of a segment of the chest wall (moves IN during inspiration, OUT during expiration, opposite to normal) is seen and felt on examination. X-ray/CT may show rib fractures but diagnosis is clinical.
Q4Correct sequence in resuscitation of trauma patient in casualtyS-16
✓ aAirway → Breathing → Circulation — ATLS primary survey: A-B-C-D-E. Airway first (life lost fastest from airway obstruction), then Breathing (tension pneumothorax, open chest wounds), then Circulation (haemorrhage control, IV access). This sequence saves the most lives in polytrauma.

CH 25 Emergency Neurosurgery
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Cerebrospinal otorrhoea is caused byM-04
✓ aFracture of middle fossa — CSF otorrhoea (CSF leaking from the ear) is caused by fracture of the petrous part of the temporal bone (middle cranial fossa fracture), which tears the dura and tympanic membrane. CSF rhinorrhoea = anterior cranial fossa fracture (cribriform plate).
Q2Lucid interval is seen inN-08
✓ cExtradural haemorrhage — Classic lucid interval: unconscious → regains consciousness → becomes unconscious again. This occurs in EXTRADURAL haematoma (EDH) due to tearing of the middle meningeal artery. The initial LOC is from concussion, then as the haematoma expands, raised ICP causes the second deterioration.
Q3Earliest manifestation of increased intracranial tension following head injuryN-09, W-14
✓ aIpsilateral pupillary dilatation — As ICP rises, the uncus of the temporal lobe herniates downward through the tentorium, compressing the ipsilateral CN III (oculomotor nerve). This causes ipsilateral pupil dilatation (dilated, unreactive = "blown pupil"). This is the EARLIEST sign of transtentorial herniation.
Note: The book marks (a) as correct. Altered mental status is also an early sign but pupillary dilatation is more specific and pathognomonic of transtentorial herniation.
Q4RTA patient: transient LOC → regained consciousness → again unconscious. Most likely diagnosisN-09
✓ bSubdural haemorrhage — The book marks subdural haemorrhage here. However: the CLASSIC lucid interval is EDH. Subdural haematoma (especially acute-on-chronic) can also present with gradual deterioration after a lucid period. Note: Q2 (lucid interval = EDH) and this Q4 (subdural) appear contradictory — the classic teaching assigns lucid interval to EDH, but some texts describe a similar presentation with subdural. Trust the book's marked answer for exam purposes: subdural for this clinical scenario.
Q5Maximum score in Glasgow Coma Scale isN-09
✓ d15 — GCS maximum = 15 (E4 + V5 + M6). GCS minimum = 3 (not 0). Scoring: Eye opening (E): 1–4, Verbal (V): 1–5, Motor (M): 1–6. GCS ≤8 = severe TBI (intubation indicated). GCS 9–12 = moderate. GCS 13–15 = mild.
Q6Features of raised intracranial tension EXCEPTM-10, W-12
✓ dTachycardia — Raised ICP causes BRADYCARDIA (not tachycardia), as part of the Cushing's triad: (1) Hypertension, (2) Bradycardia, (3) Irregular respirations. The triad represents a terminal attempt by the brainstem to maintain cerebral perfusion pressure. Tachycardia suggests shock/haemorrhage, not raised ICP.
Q7In Glasgow Coma Scale, all of the following are considered EXCEPTM-10, S-12, W-12
✓ dSensory response — GCS assesses 3 parameters ONLY: (1) Eye opening (E), (2) Verbal response (V), (3) Motor response (M). Sensory/pain response is NOT separately scored in GCS. Pain is used as a STIMULUS to elicit motor and eye-opening responses, but sensory modality itself is not scored.
Q8Laceration of a branch of the middle meningeal artery leads toN-10
✓ aExtradural haematoma — The middle meningeal artery runs in a groove on the inner surface of the temporal bone. Temporal bone fracture tears this artery → arterial bleeding accumulates between the skull and the dura (extradural space) → biconvex (lens-shaped) haematoma on CT. Needs emergency craniotomy.
Q9Battle's sign is indicative of fracture ofN-10
✓ bMiddle cranial fossa — Battle's sign = ecchymosis (bruising) over the mastoid process behind the ear, appearing 24–48 hours after a basilar skull fracture involving the middle cranial fossa (petrous temporal bone). Signs of base of skull fracture: Battle's sign (middle fossa), Raccoon eyes/periorbital ecchymosis (anterior fossa), CSF otorrhoea/rhinorrhoea, haemotympanum.
Q10Minimum Glasgow Coma Scale score isM-11
✓ c3 — GCS minimum = 3 (E1+V1+M1 = "No response" to all stimuli). Score of 0 is NOT possible because each component has a minimum of 1. A patient with no response to any stimulus still scores 3, not 0.
Q11Types of intracranial haematomas EXCEPTW-11
✓ dSubperitoneal — "Subperitoneal" is an abdominal term, NOT a type of intracranial haematoma. True intracranial haematomas: (1) Extradural (between skull and dura), (2) Subdural (between dura and arachnoid), (3) Subarachnoid (between arachnoid and pia), (4) Intracerebral (within brain parenchyma).
Q12Facial nerve palsy is seen in fractures ofS-13
✓ bMiddle cranial fossa — CN VII (facial nerve) runs through the petrous temporal bone (middle fossa). Fractures of the petrous temporal bone (middle cranial fossa) can injure the facial nerve in the facial canal, causing facial palsy. Also: CN VIII damage causes sensorineural deafness/vestibular disturbance.
Q13Lucid interval can be a presentation in which type of head injury?W-13
✓ aExtradural haematoma — Consistent with Q2. Lucid interval is CLASSIC for EDH. The injury is initially mild (concussion), patient wakes up, then as the arterial haematoma expands and ICP rises, the patient deteriorates again. Timing: minutes to hours after injury.
Q14Cause of extradural haemorrhage is injury toS-14
✓ dMiddle meningeal artery — The middle meningeal artery (branch of maxillary artery, enters skull at foramen spinosum) is the most common vessel injured in EDH (~85%). Venous EDH from dural sinuses can also occur. The arterial nature explains the rapid expansion and urgent surgical need.
Q15Features of anterior cranial fossa fracture EXCEPTS-14
✓ bEcchymosis posterior to mastoid process — Mastoid ecchymosis (Battle's sign) = MIDDLE cranial fossa fracture. Anterior cranial fossa fracture features: periorbital ecchymosis (raccoon/panda eyes), subconjunctival haemorrhage (no posterior limit), CSF rhinorrhoea (from cribriform plate), epistaxis, anosmia (CN I damage), and unilateral blindness (CN II).
Q16According to GCS, a verbal score of 1 indicatesW-14
✓ aNo response — GCS Verbal scoring: V5 = Oriented, V4 = Confused/Disoriented, V3 = Inappropriate words, V2 = Incomprehensible sounds, V1 = No verbal response. Score of 1 = absolute silence. Score of 2 = groaning. Score of 3 = single words out of context.
Q17Most important clinical finding in a case of head injury isS-15
✓ bLevel of consciousness — The level of consciousness (GCS) is the SINGLE MOST IMPORTANT clinical parameter in head injury. It guides management decisions (intubation at GCS ≤8), monitors deterioration, and determines prognosis. Serial GCS monitoring is essential in all head injuries.
Q18All of the following lower intracranial pressure EXCEPTS-15
✓ cCorticosteroids — Corticosteroids do NOT lower ICP in TBI (traumatic brain injury) — they are specifically CONTRAINDICATED in TBI (CRASH trial showed increased mortality). They DO lower ICP in cerebral oedema from tumours/abscesses. Methods to lower ICP: Mannitol (osmotic diuretic), Frusemide (diuretic), Hyperventilation (reduce PaCO₂ → vasoconstriction), head elevation, CSF drainage.
Q19Management of extradural haematoma isW-15
✓ bImmediate evacuation — EDH is a neurosurgical emergency requiring IMMEDIATE craniotomy and evacuation of the haematoma. Time is brain — delay beyond 2–4 hours significantly worsens outcomes. A burr hole may be done if craniotomy is not immediately available. Observation alone is NOT acceptable for symptomatic EDH.
Q20Common vessel to rupture in extradural haematoma isW-17
✓ aMeningeal artery (Middle meningeal artery) — Consistent with Q14. The middle meningeal artery is the most commonly torn vessel in EDH (~85% of cases). It runs in the epidural space in a groove on the temporal bone, making it vulnerable to temporal bone fractures.
Q21In Glasgow Coma Scale, all the following are considered EXCEPTS-18
✓ dSensory response — Repeat of Q7. GCS = Eye opening + Verbal response + Motor response. Sensory response is NOT a GCS component.
Q22Minimum score in Glasgow Coma Scale isS-18
✓ b3 — Repeat of Q10. Minimum GCS = 3 (E1+V1+M1). Not zero. Important exam fact: GCS range = 3–15, NOT 0–15.
Q23Best prognostic factor for head injury isW-19
✓ cMode of injury — The book marks "mode of injury" (c) as the answer. However, clinically the GCS is the most widely used prognostic tool. The mode/mechanism of injury (RTA vs fall vs assault, velocity, penetrating vs blunt) determines the force delivered to the brain and strongly predicts outcome. For exam purposes, follow the book's marked answer: mode of injury.

CH 27 Maxillofacial Trauma
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1The commonest site of fracture of mandible isN-10
✓ aCondylar neck — The condylar neck/subcondylar region is the most common site of mandibular fracture (~36%), due to its thin cross-section and indirect force transmission. Other sites in order: angle of mandible (~20%), symphysis/parasymphysis (~17%). The condyle acts as a "crumple zone."
Q2Best view of mandible isW-15
✓ dOrthopentogram (OPG) — Orthopantomogram (OPG/DPT) gives a panoramic view of the entire mandible, temporomandibular joints, and lower teeth in a single image. It is the GOLD STANDARD radiograph for mandibular fracture assessment, showing all sites including the condyle.
Q3Patient: inability to close mouth after yawning. Diagnosis?S-16
✓ dSubluxation of temporomandibular joint — Wide opening of the mouth (as in yawning) can cause the condyle to displace ANTERIOR to the articular eminence of the TMJ, locking in open position. The masseter and temporalis go into spasm, preventing mouth closure. Reduction: downward pressure on molars + backward push. Tetanus/masseter spasm causes trismus (cannot OPEN mouth, opposite).
CH 28 Torso Trauma
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Bleeding from cystic artery is best controlled by the maneuver ofN-03
✓ aPringle manoeuvre — Pringle manoeuvre = manual compression of the hepatoduodenal ligament between thumb and index finger (or with a non-crushing clamp), temporarily occluding the portal vein and hepatic artery to reduce hepatic blood flow and control haemorrhage from liver, cystic artery, or bile duct injuries. Named after James Hogarth Pringle (1908).
Q2Weighing of swabs post-operatively is a method to assessN-08
✓ cBlood loss — Gravimetric method: pre-weighed dry swabs weighed again after use. Difference in weight (g) ≈ blood volume lost (mL) since blood density ≈ 1 g/mL. Most accurate method of intraoperative blood loss estimation.
Q3Investigation of choice for blunt abdominal trauma in an UNSTABLE patient isN-09
✓ cCT Scan — Note: The book marks CT scan, but current ATLS guidelines recommend FAST (bedside ultrasound) for haemodynamically UNSTABLE patients because it is immediate and non-invasive. CT scan is for STABLE patients. DPL is an older alternative. If the exam book marks CT, follow for MUHS exam purposes. Clinically: FAST for unstable, CT for stable patients with blunt abdominal trauma.
Q4Pringle's manoeuvre is to stop bleeding fromN-09
✓ bHepatic artery (liver) — Pringle compresses the hepatoduodenal ligament, which contains: portal vein (posterior), hepatic artery (left), common bile duct (right). It controls inflow to the liver. Used in liver trauma and hepatic surgery to temporarily stop haemorrhage from the liver and its vessels.
Q5Most common organ to rupture following blunt abdominal trauma isM-10
✓ aSpleen — The spleen is the most commonly injured solid organ in blunt abdominal trauma (~40–55% of solid organ injuries), due to its location, size, and fragile parenchyma. The liver is the second most common. Signs: left upper quadrant pain, Kehr's sign (referred left shoulder pain), haemodynamic instability.
Q6Kehr's sign is associated withN-10
✓ bSplenic rupture — Kehr's sign = referred pain to the LEFT shoulder tip (from left phrenic nerve irritation by subdiaphragmatic blood pooling from a ruptured spleen). It is more prominent when the patient is supine with legs raised (Trendelenburg position forces blood superiorly). Classic sign of haemoperitoneum from splenic injury.
Q7Tachypnoea, dyspnoea and neck veins distension constitutesM-11
✓ bTension Pneumothorax — Classic triad: tachypnoea/dyspnoea + distended neck veins (JVD from mediastinal shift → obstructed SVC return) + tracheal deviation away from affected side + absent breath sounds. Also: hypotension (obstructive shock). Treatment: immediate needle decompression (2nd ICS MCL), then chest drain.
Q8Pericardial aspiration is usually done throughM-11
✓ aSubxiphoid region — Pericardiocentesis is performed via the subxiphoid approach: needle inserted at 45° angle between xiphoid process and left costal margin, directed toward left shoulder. ECG-guided or echo-guided. Used for cardiac tamponade (Beck's triad: hypotension, JVD, muffled heart sounds).
Q9All are indications of urgent thoracotomy EXCEPTW-11
✓ c150 mL blood in the pleural space — 150 mL is a SMALL haemothorax — managed conservatively (observation ± intercostal drain). Indications for urgent thoracotomy: initial drainage >1500 mL blood, ongoing haemorrhage >200 mL/hr for 3–4 hours, massive haemothorax, cardiac tamponade not responding to pericardiocentesis, oesophageal injury, aortic injury.
Q10Diagnosis of flail chest is done byW-13
✓ aClinical examination — Repeat of Ch.24 Q3. Flail chest = paradoxical chest wall movement = CLINICAL diagnosis. Defined as fracture of ≥3 consecutive ribs in ≥2 places, creating a free-floating "flail segment." The underlying pulmonary contusion is the real danger.
Q11Triangle of safety for intercostal drain insertion does NOT includeW-13
✓ bAnterior to midaxillary line — The "triangle of safety" for ICD insertion: APEX = axilla, ANTERIOR border = anterior axillary line (NOT midaxillary), POSTERIOR border = posterior axillary line, INFERIOR border = level of nipple (5th ICS). Insertion is POSTERIOR to the anterior axillary line (i.e., in the midaxillary/posterior axillary region). "Anterior to midaxillary line" is outside the safe triangle.
Q12Man with multiple rib fractures and paradoxical chest movement. Management isS-15
✓ dIntermittent Positive Pressure Ventilation (IPPV) — Management of flail chest: IPPV (internal pneumatic stabilisation) is the definitive treatment for large flail segments with respiratory failure. It splints the chest from inside. Strapping is CONTRAINDICATED (worsens respiratory failure). Analgesia, physiotherapy, and high-flow O₂ for smaller flail segments.
Q13Blunt abdominal trauma with tachycardia, hypotension, pallor. Most likely injury?S-16
✓ aSplenic injury — Haemodynamic instability (tachycardia + hypotension + pallor = haemorrhagic shock) after blunt abdominal trauma = splenic injury until proven otherwise. The spleen is the most commonly injured organ in blunt abdominal trauma. Kehr's sign, left flank tenderness support the diagnosis.
Q1450-yr-old in shock with haemoperitoneum: emergency laparotomy shows lacerations at three sites on spleen. Better option?S-16
✓ bSplenectomy — Multiple lacerations at 3 sites (Grade IV–V injury) in a haemodynamically unstable patient = SPLENECTOMY. Splenorrhaphy (repair) is for low-grade injuries in stable patients. Packing is a damage control option. Post-splenectomy: vaccinate against encapsulated organisms (pneumococcus, meningococcus, Haemophilus) and lifelong penicillin prophylaxis.
Q15Pringle manoeuvre is used during surgical management of traumatic injury ofW-16
✓ bLiver — Pringle's manoeuvre temporarily controls hepatic inflow (portal vein + hepatic artery) during liver surgery/trauma. It reduces hepatic blood flow to allow haemostasis of liver lacerations. Safe for up to 15–20 minutes (warm ischaemia). Can be extended with intermittent clamping.
Q16Kehr's sign in splenic rupture isW-16
✓ aPain over left shoulder — Repeat of Q6. Blood under the left hemidiaphragm irritates the diaphragm → referred pain to left shoulder tip via left phrenic nerve (C3,4,5). Classic sign of splenic rupture/haemoperitoneum in left upper quadrant.
Q17Flail chest isW-17
✓ bParadoxic movement of chest wall — Flail chest = segment of chest wall moves PARADOXICALLY: inward during inspiration (as intrathoracic pressure falls), outward during expiration. Caused by fracture of ≥3 consecutive ribs in ≥2 places. The paradox is due to loss of bony continuity with the rest of the thoracic cage.
Q18Large segment flail chest with hypoxia. Treatment of choiceS-17
✓ aIPPV (Intermittent Positive Pressure Ventilation) — Consistent with Q12. IPPV is definitive treatment for large flail chest with respiratory compromise. It provides "internal pneumatic splinting" — the positive pressure keeps the lung expanded and stabilises the flail segment from within.
Q19All are indications of thoracotomy EXCEPTS-18
✓ bPulmonary contusion — Pulmonary contusion (bruising of lung parenchyma) is managed conservatively with mechanical ventilation if needed, NOT thoracotomy. Indications for thoracotomy: massive haemothorax, major bronchus tear, oesophageal rupture, cardiac tamponade refractory to pericardiocentesis, ongoing haemorrhage >200 mL/hr.
Q20Post-splenectomy: vaccinate forS-18
✓ aPneumococcus — Post-splenectomy vaccination is essential against encapsulated bacteria (OPSI — Overwhelming Post-Splenectomy Infection risk). Vaccines required: (1) Pneumococcal (Streptococcus pneumoniae) — most important, (2) Meningococcal (Neisseria meningitidis), (3) Haemophilus influenzae type b. Vaccinate ≥2 weeks before elective splenectomy or ASAP after emergency splenectomy.
Q21RTA: respiratory distress, hypotension, emphysema, no air entry on one side. Next best step?PG 2020
✓ bNeedle decompression in the 2nd ICS (book says 5th ICS — note discrepancy) — Clinical picture = Tension Pneumothorax (subcutaneous emphysema + absent breath sounds + hypotension + respiratory distress). IMMEDIATE needle decompression is first-line emergency treatment — 2nd ICS midclavicular line (standard) or 5th ICS anterior axillary line (safe triangle). Do NOT wait for X-ray. Then insert ICD.
Standard teaching: needle decompression at 2nd ICS MCL. The book states 5th ICS — follow book for MUHS exam.
Q22Stab injury lower chest: low pulses, BP improved with IV fluids, CXR = clear lungs. Next step?PG 2020
✓ dE-FAST — Clear lungs on CXR + stab to lower chest + haemodynamic instability = suspect CARDIAC TAMPONADE or abdominal injury. E-FAST (Extended FAST) = bedside ultrasound examining pericardium (for tamponade), peritoneal cavity (haemoperitoneum), and pleural spaces. It is the next most appropriate bedside investigation in this scenario.
Q23Left hypochondrium contusion, SBP 70 mmHg, PR 110. What to do next?PG 2021
✓ aFAST — Haemodynamically UNSTABLE patient (SBP 70, HR 110 = Class III shock) with left hypochondrium contusion (suspect splenic injury). FAST (Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma) is the immediate bedside investigation of choice. If FAST positive (haemoperitoneum) → emergency laparotomy. CECT requires a stable patient. DPL is older and invasive.

CH 30 Burns
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Burns involving the front of chest and abdomen in an adult account forM-03
✓ b18% — Rule of Nines (Wallace, adults): Head & neck = 9%, Each arm = 9%, Anterior trunk = 18% (chest 9% + abdomen 9%), Posterior trunk = 18%, Each leg = 18%, Perineum = 1%. Front of chest + abdomen = ANTERIOR TRUNK = 18%.
Rule of Nines mnemonic: "9 for head, 9 each arm, 18 front, 18 back, 18 each leg, 1 perineum" = 99% + 1% = 100%
Q2Third degree burns are characterised byM-04
✓ dLoss of sensations and anaesthesia — Full-thickness (3rd degree) burns destroy ALL layers of skin including sensory nerve endings → anaesthesia (PAINLESS). Paradox: the deepest burns are PAINLESS while partial-thickness burns are extremely painful. Also: leathery/waxy/charred appearance, no blistering, no capillary refill.
Q3Frostbite is caused byN-08
✓ cExposure to extreme cold — Frostbite = tissue injury from freezing (ice crystal formation in cells → cell membrane rupture + vascular damage + thrombosis). Affects extremities (digits, ears, nose). Grades: frostnip → superficial → partial-thickness → full-thickness. NOT caused by bites.
Q4Burns involving the head and neck in a child aged 1 year accounts forN-10
✓ c18% — In children, the Rule of Nines is MODIFIED (Lund and Browder chart). An infant's head is proportionally larger (18% at age 1 vs 9% in adults). Each percentage point taken from head is added to legs. At age 1: Head = 18%, Each leg = 13.5%. Use Lund-Browder chart for accurate paediatric BSA estimation.
Q5Different types of surgical dressings areM-11
✓ dAll of the above — Modern wound dressings include all types: bead dressings/polymeric films (transparent, permeable), hydrocolloids, fibrous polymers (alginate, carboxymethylcellulose), hydrogels, and foams. Each is suited to different wound types (dry vs exudating, infected vs clean).
Q6Following is NOT a major determinant of outcome of Burns caseW-11
✓ aH/o suicidal/homicidal burns — The CAUSE (suicidal vs accidental vs homicidal) does NOT determine the physiological outcome of burns. Major determinants of burns outcome: (1) % BSA burned (Baux score = %BSA + age), (2) Depth of burns, (3) Presence of inhalation injury (dramatically worsens prognosis), (4) Age, (5) Associated injuries.
Q7In early local management of burnsS-12
✓ bWounds should be meticulously cleansed and devitalised tissue excised — Early burn wound management: Cool the burn (cold running water ≤20 min if <3hrs post-burn), then clean with antiseptic, debride non-viable tissue, and cover. Superficial burns CAN be left exposed (exposure method). Full-thickness burns need early excision and grafting, not daily dressing.
Q8Which of the following is NOT an indication for hospitalisation?S-13
✓ a5% burns in child — 5% superficial burns in a child does NOT require hospitalisation in isolation. Indications for admission: >10% BSA burns (any age), >5% full-thickness, burns to face/hands/feet/genitalia/perineum/major joints, inhalation injury, electrical burns (always admit), chemical burns, circumferential burns, extremes of age (<5 or >60), significant associated injuries.
Q9Curling's ulcer is seen inS-13
✓ aBurn patient — Curling's ulcer = acute peptic ulcer (duodenal/gastric) occurring as a stress response in burn patients. Mechanism: severe physiologic stress → elevated cortisol/catecholamines → reduced mucosal blood flow → ischaemia → ulceration. Cushing's ulcer = head injury patients (vagal stimulation → increased acid).
Memory: Curling (burns) vs Cushing (head injury/neurosurgery)
Q10Most common cause of death due to burn in early period isS-13
✓ aHypovolemic shock — In the EARLY period (<48–72 hours): massive plasma loss → hypovolaemic shock is the leading cause of death. In the LATE period (>48–72 hours): sepsis/multi-organ failure becomes the main cause. Hence early aggressive fluid resuscitation (Parkland formula) is critical.
Q11Most important aspect of management of burn injury in first 24 hoursS-15
✓ aFluid resuscitation — Aggressive IV fluid resuscitation is the cornerstone of early burn management. The Parkland formula guides fluid replacement. Goals: urine output 0.5–1 mL/kg/hr (adults), 1 mL/kg/hr (children). Failure to resuscitate leads to hypovolaemic shock and organ failure. Ringer's Lactate is the preferred fluid.
Q12Rule of nine to estimate surface area of burnt patient was introduced byS-15
✓ bAlexander Wallace — The "Rule of Nines" was introduced by Alexander Wallace in 1951. It divides the body surface into regions each representing approximately 9% (or multiples thereof) for adults. More accurate Lund-Browder chart used for children. Wallace's rule is quick and practical for emergency assessment.
Q13Late death in burns is due toW-15
✓ aSepsis — Late burn mortality (>72 hours onwards): SEPSIS / multi-organ dysfunction syndrome (MODS) is the leading cause. Burn wounds are ideal culture medium for bacteria (Pseudomonas, MRSA, Candida). Loss of skin barrier + immunosuppression → systemic infection. Consistent with Q10 (early = hypovolaemia; late = sepsis).
Q14Parkland formula for calculation of fluid requirement in burn patients isW-16
✓ a4 mL × weight (kg) × % BSA burned = total volume (mL) in first 24 hours — Parkland formula: 4 mL × kg × %TBSA burn = total Ringer's Lactate in 24 hours. Give ½ in first 8 hours (from time of burn, not admission), ½ in next 16 hours. Children: add maintenance fluids (D5RL). Modified Brooke = 2 mL × kg × %BSA.

Quick Reference: Key Mnemonics & High-Yield Facts

🧠 GCS: E(1–4) + V(1–5) + M(1–6) = range 3–15. Intubate at ≤8. Minimum = 3, not 0.
🔥 Burns: Early death = Hypovolaemia. Late death = Sepsis. Parkland = 4×kg×%BSA (RL, 24h).
🩸 Shock classes: I<15%, II 15–30%, III 30–40%, IV>40%. Best assessed by urine output.
💊 Longest LA = Bupivacaine. LA fails in infection = ↓ pH.
🔬 GCS components: Eye + Verbal + Motor (NOT sensory).
🦷 EDH = middle meningeal artery + lucid interval. SDH = bridging veins + elderly/alcoholics.
🧪 TPN: subclavian vein, no fibres, hyperglycaemia (not hypoglycaemia) is the complication.
🌡️ Wound rupture warning = serosanguinous discharge on day 7–10.
