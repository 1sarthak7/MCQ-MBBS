CH 43 Elective Neurosurgery
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1All are brilliantly transilluminant swellings EXCEPTW-12
✓ bDermoid cyst — Dermoid cysts contain solid elements (hair, teeth, keratin, sebum) — NOT brilliantly transilluminant. Brilliantly transilluminant: Cystic hygroma (lymphangioma), Meningocele (CSF-filled), Cyst of epididymis, Hydrocele. Repeat from Ch. 42.
Q2The most common intracerebral tumours areS-14
✓ aGliomas — Gliomas (astrocytomas, glioblastoma multiforme, oligodendrogliomas) are the most common PRIMARY brain tumours (~30% of all brain tumours, ~80% of malignant primary brain tumours). GBM (Grade IV astrocytoma) is the most common malignant type. Meningiomas are more common overall but are extra-axial, not intracerebral.
Q3Which of the following brain tumours is highly vascular in nature?W-14
✓ aGlioblastoma multiforme (GBM) — GBM (Grade IV astrocytoma) is highly vascular with neovascularisation, pseudopalisading necrosis, and endothelial proliferation. Produces VEGF in large amounts. On imaging: ring-enhancing lesion with central necrosis and surrounding oedema. Worst prognosis of all brain tumours (median survival ~15 months).
CH 44 The Eye and Orbit
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Dermoid arises fromW-15
✓ bTotipotent cell — Dermoid cysts arise from totipotent cells (ectodermal cells) trapped along lines of embryonic fusion during development. They contain elements from all three germ layers potentially. The "totipotent cell" theory explains why dermoids contain diverse structures (skin, hair, teeth, sebaceous glands).
Q2Which cyst generally develops in the line of embryonic fusion?W-16
✓ bDermoid cyst — Dermoid cysts develop at the LINES OF EMBRYONIC FUSION (sites where embryonic folds/processes fuse during development). Common sites: outer angle of orbit (external angular dermoid — most common), root of nose, midline scalp, presacral. This distinguishes them from sebaceous cysts (blocked follicles) and thyroglossal cysts (thyroid descent).
Q3Melanoma can arise from all sites EXCEPTS-16
✓ bSpleen — Repeat from Ch. 42. Melanocytes (neural crest-derived) are present in: skin, retina/uveal tract, meninges, mucosal surfaces. The spleen has no melanocytes — melanoma found in the spleen is always metastatic, never primary.
CH 45 Cleft Lip and Palate
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Cleft lip is due to non-fusion ofN-03
✓ cMaxillary and medial nasal process — Cleft lip results from failure of fusion between the MAXILLARY PROCESS and the MEDIAL NASAL PROCESS (fronto-nasal process) during the 5th–8th week of embryonic development. The philtrum and premaxilla are derived from the medial nasal process. Failure of mesenchymal migration/fusion → cleft lip.
Embryology: Upper lip = maxillary process (×2) + medial nasal process (×1, central). Lower lip = two mandibular processes. Cleft palate = failure of palatine shelf fusion.
Q2Cleft lip repair is commonly performed at the age ofN-10
✓ b3–6 months — Rule of Ten for cleft lip repair: weight ≥10 lbs (4.5 kg), Hb ≥10 g/dL, age ≥10 weeks (approximately 3 months). Surgery is typically performed at 3–6 months. Cleft palate repair is done later at 9–18 months (to allow palatal growth but before speech develops, ideally before 12–18 months).
CH 46 Head and Neck Swellings and Surgery
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Commonest site of thyroglossal cyst isM-03
✓ cSub-hyoid (infrahyoid) — The most common site of thyroglossal cyst is SUBHYOID (below the hyoid bone), accounting for ~65–75% of cases. The thyroid gland descends from the foramen caecum at the tongue base → through the hyoid → to final position. Cysts can form anywhere along this tract but are most common just below the hyoid.
Q2Referred pain to the ear is commonly seen inM-03
✓ cCarcinoma of nasopharynx — Referred otalgia (ear pain) from head and neck malignancies occurs via shared nerve supply. Carcinoma of the nasopharynx, base of tongue, tonsil, pharynx, and larynx can all cause referred otalgia via CN IX (Jacobson's nerve) and CN X (Arnold's nerve). Nasopharyngeal carcinoma specifically causes: unilateral otalgia, nasal obstruction, epistaxis, cervical lymphadenopathy, and cranial nerve palsies.
Q3The term chemodectoma denotes tumour involvingN-03
✓ cCarotid bodies — Chemodectoma = tumour of chemoreceptor tissue (paraganglioma). The carotid body (at the carotid bifurcation) is the most common site — "carotid body tumour." Other sites: jugulotympanic (glomus jugulare), vagal body. Features: slow-growing, pulsatile neck mass at carotid bifurcation, splaying of ICA and ECA on angiography ("Lyre sign"). Treatment: surgical excision.
Q4The best treatment for cystic hygroma isN-09
✓ cSurgical excision — Cystic hygroma (lymphatic malformation/lymphangioma) is best treated by SURGICAL EXCISION, though complete excision is difficult due to infiltration of surrounding structures. Modern alternatives: sclerotherapy with OK-432 (Picibanil) or bleomycin have good results, particularly for macrocystic types. Radiotherapy and chemotherapy are NOT used.
Q5Trotter's Triad is seen inM-10
✓ bNasopharyngeal carcinoma — Trotter's triad (Trotter's syndrome): (1) Ipsilateral conductive deafness (Eustachian tube involvement), (2) Immobility of the ipsilateral soft palate (pterygoid/palatal muscle infiltration), (3) Ipsilateral trigeminal neuralgia (CN V involvement through skull base). Caused by nasopharyngeal carcinoma invading the lateral wall of the nasopharynx.
Q6Brilliantly transilluminant swelling in the neck isM-10
✓ aCystic Hygroma — Cystic hygroma (lymphangioma) is the classic BRILLIANTLY TRANSILLUMINANT neck swelling because it is filled with clear lymph fluid in thin-walled cysts. Present at birth or infancy, soft, compressible, posterior triangle. Branchial cyst partially transilluminates; thyroglossal and dermoid do not.
Q7Radical dissection of the neck includes surgical removal of all EXCEPTM-10
✓ cPhrenic nerves — Radical neck dissection (RND) removes: ALL cervical lymph nodes (levels I–V), sternocleidomastoid muscle, internal jugular vein, submandibular gland, tail of parotid, and spinal accessory nerve (CN XI). The PHRENIC NERVE (C3,4,5) is NOT routinely removed — its sacrifice would cause ipsilateral diaphragm paralysis.
Q8Structures preserved in modified radical neck dissection areM-11
✓ cInternal jugular vein + accessory nerve + sternocleidomastoid muscle — Modified Radical Neck Dissection (MRND) removes all 5 levels of cervical lymph nodes (same as RND) but PRESERVES one or more of: (1) CN XI (spinal accessory nerve), (2) Internal jugular vein, (3) Sternocleidomastoid muscle. Type III MRND preserves all three — most commonly performed type.
Q9The most common site of thyroglossal cyst isS-12
✓ bSubhyoid — Repeat of Q1. Subhyoid = ~65–75%. Suprahyoid = ~20%. At foramen caecum (lingual) = ~5%. Infrahyoid near isthmus of thyroid = less common. Key sign: moves on swallowing AND on tongue protrusion (attached to hyoid via tract).
Q10The following is included in the differential diagnosis of midline neck swellingsS-12
✓ dBranchial cyst — Wait — branchial cyst is a LATERAL neck swelling, NOT midline. The book answer appears to be "d) branchial cyst" but this is surprising. Midline neck swellings: thyroglossal cyst, submental lymph nodes, dermoid cyst, thyroid swelling, laryngocele (can be midline), lipoma. Branchial cyst = anterior triangle lateral to SCM = lateral neck. This may be a book error. For MUHS exam purposes, follow the book answer: d.
Q11All are brilliantly transilluminant swellings EXCEPTW-12
✓ bDermoid cyst — Repeat. Dermoid cyst = NOT transilluminant (solid contents). This question appears multiple times in the question bank — it is a very high-yield fact.
Q12Cervical lymph nodes are classified into _____ levelsW-12
✓ a5 levels (I–V) — Cervical lymph nodes (Robbins classification): Level I = submental + submandibular, Level II = upper jugular, Level III = middle jugular, Level IV = lower jugular, Level V = posterior triangle. Some classifications add Level VI (central compartment/pretracheal) and Level VII (superior mediastinal). Standard = 5 or 6 levels depending on source.
Q13Following are the incisions for appendicectomy EXCEPTS-12
✓ dKocher's incision — Kocher's (subcostal) incision is for the RIGHT UPPER QUADRANT — used for cholecystectomy and hepatic surgery, NOT appendicectomy. Appendicectomy incisions: McBurney's (standard oblique over McBurney's point), Lanz (transverse/cosmetic), Rutherford-Morrison (muscle-cutting, for obese patients/difficult access).
Q14The commonest position for thyroglossal cyst isS-14
✓ bSubhyoid — Third time this appears. Subhyoid is the most common site (~65–75%). High-yield exam fact: thyroglossal cyst moves with tongue protrusion AND swallowing. Treatment: Sistrunk operation (excision of cyst + hyoid body middle third + thyroglossal tract to foramen caecum).
Q15The nodes most commonly involved in tuberculosis areS-14
✓ aCervical lymph nodes — Cervical lymphadenopathy is the most common form of peripheral tuberculous lymphadenitis (scrofula). Upper deep cervical and posterior triangle nodes are most commonly affected. Classic feature: multiple nodes, matted, cold abscess formation → collar stud abscess → sinus. Histology: caseating granuloma with Langhans giant cells.
Q16True about Branchial cyst isW-14
✓ bMostly arises from 2nd branchial system — ~95% of branchial cysts arise from the 2nd branchial arch/cleft. Located at the anterior border of SCM, junction of upper and middle thirds. Contents: clear fluid with cholesterol crystals, lined by stratified squamous epithelium. Cysts are more common than sinuses. Treatment: surgical excision (not always mandatory for sinuses).
Q17Vital structure preserved during standard radical neck dissectionS-15, W-15
✓ aVagus nerve — The VAGUS NERVE (CN X) is ALWAYS preserved in both radical and modified radical neck dissection. It runs in the carotid sheath between the carotid artery and internal jugular vein. Sacrificing the vagus nerve causes: ipsilateral vocal cord paralysis, hoarseness, dysphagia, and cardiac arrhythmias.
Q18Malignant cell in Hodgkin's lymphoma isS-15, W-15
✓ bReed-Sternberg cell — Wait, the book marks "b) Lymphocyte" but the correct answer clinically is REED-STERNBERG CELL. Reed-Sternberg cells (large binucleate/multinucleate cells with prominent "owl-eye" nucleoli) are the PATHOGNOMONIC malignant cells of Hodgkin's lymphoma. The book's answer "Lymphocyte" appears to be an error. For clinical purposes: Reed-Sternberg. Follow book (b) for MUHS exam.
Correct clinical answer: Reed-Sternberg cell. Book marks Lymphocyte — this is likely a printing error. Remember for all exams: RS cell = Hodgkin's lymphoma hallmark.
Q19Carotid artery is palpated against transverse process tubercle of which vertebra?S-16
✓ aC6 (Chassaignac's tubercle) — The carotid artery can be compressed against the ANTERIOR TUBERCLE of the transverse process of C6 (Chassaignac's tubercle) to control bleeding or assess pulse. C6 anterior tubercle is the most prominent and easily palpable carotid compression point in the neck.
Q201-year-old child: posterior triangle neck swelling, brilliantly transilluminant. Diagnosis?S-16
✓ dCystic hygroma — Classic presentation: infant/child + posterior triangle + brilliantly transilluminant = CYSTIC HYGROMA (lymphatic malformation). Cystic hygroma is the most common cause of brilliantly transilluminant swelling in the posterior triangle of the neck in children.
Q21Which level of cervical lymph nodes are NOT removed in Supra-omohyoid neck dissection?S-16
✓ dLevel IV — Supra-omohyoid neck dissection (SOHND) is a selective neck dissection removing levels I, II, and III only (above the omohyoid muscle). Level IV (lower jugular) and Level V (posterior triangle) are NOT removed. Used for oral cavity cancers (N0 neck) where risk of Level IV/V metastasis is low.
Q22All structures removed in radical neck dissection EXCEPTW-17
✓ dTrapezius muscle — Trapezius muscle is NOT removed in radical neck dissection. RND removes: all 5 levels of cervical lymph nodes, SCM, IJV, CN XI (spinal accessory nerve), submandibular gland, tail of parotid. The trapezius is preserved. Sacrificing CN XI causes trapezius weakness (shoulder drop) but the muscle itself is not excised.
Q23Carotid body tumourW-19
✓ bOriginates from Schwann cells — Carotid body tumour (paraganglioma/chemodectoma) arises from CHIEF CELLS (type I glomus cells) and sustentacular cells (type II = modified Schwann cells). The book marks "Schwann cells." Features: slow-growing pulsatile neck mass, "lyre sign" on angiography (splaying of ICA/ECA), may metastasise in ~5%. Surgery is the treatment of choice.

CH 49 Oropharyngeal Cancer
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1The most common cancer in India isN-09
✓ bCA Breast — Breast cancer is the most common cancer in women in India and now the most common cancer overall in India (surpassing oral/cervical cancer). However, oral cavity cancer is still the most common cancer in Indian MALES. The book marks CA Breast for this question (N-09). CA Oral Cavity is most common in men; CA Cervix was previously #1 in women but now CA Breast leads.
Q2Features of treatment of oral cancer EXCEPTW-11
✓ dIntravenous antibiotics — IV antibiotics are NOT a treatment modality for oral cancer. Oral cancer treatment: (1) Surgery (resection ± reconstruction with flaps), (2) Radiotherapy (external beam ± brachytherapy), (3) Chemotherapy (cisplatin, 5-FU — as sensitiser with RT or for metastatic disease). Antibiotics treat infection, NOT cancer.
Q3The commonest site for carcinoma tongue isW-12, W-17
✓ dLateral aspect in anterior 2/3rd — The lateral border (edge) of the anterior 2/3rd of the tongue is the MOST COMMON site for tongue carcinoma. Mechanical irritation from sharp teeth and tobacco chewing exposure is highest here. The posterior 1/3rd (base of tongue) is considered oropharynx and has different biology/treatment.
Q4Which is NOT true of carcinoma tongue?S-13
✓ aCommonly adenocarcinoma — Carcinoma of the tongue is almost exclusively SQUAMOUS CELL CARCINOMA (SCC), NOT adenocarcinoma. Adenocarcinoma arises from minor salivary glands (rare). All other options are TRUE: lateral border involvement, cervical lymph node metastasis, and tobacco chewing as a risk factor.
Q5Paterson Kelly syndrome is associated with all EXCEPTW-14
✓ aDiarrhoea — Paterson-Kelly (Plummer-Vinson) syndrome = iron deficiency anaemia + glossitis + dysphagia (post-cricoid web). Diarrhoea is NOT a feature. Features: female middle-aged patients, koilonychia, angular cheilitis, glossitis, anaemia, dysphagia. Premalignant for post-cricoid carcinoma (hypopharyngeal SCC).
Q6Commonest cancer of oral cavity isW-15
✓ dSquamous cell carcinoma — >90% of oral cavity malignancies are SCC. The oral mucosa is lined by stratified squamous epithelium, making SCC the dominant cancer type. Salivary gland tumours (minor salivary glands) are rare. Melanoma can occur rarely on the oral mucosa.
Q7Commonest premalignant condition of oral cancer isS-16
✓ cLeukoplakia — Leukoplakia (white patch on oral mucosa that cannot be scraped off and cannot be attributed to any other condition) is the most common oral premalignant condition. Malignant transformation rate: 5–15% over 10 years. Erythroplakia is RARER but has HIGHER malignant potential (~40%). Risk factors: tobacco, alcohol, HPV.
Q8Ulcer over the lateral margin of tongue can be caused byS-16
✓ dAll of the above — Lateral tongue ulcers can result from: trauma (sharp teeth, ill-fitting dentures), tobacco chewing, spices (chemical irritation), SCC, TB, syphilis. In clinical practice, any non-healing lateral tongue ulcer >2–3 weeks must be biopsied to exclude SCC.
Q9Which condition is NOT associated with high risk of malignant transformation?S-16
✓ aSideropenic dysphagia (Paterson-Kelly) — While Paterson-Kelly syndrome does predispose to post-cricoid carcinoma (hypopharyngeal), the risk of malignant transformation is LOWER than erythroplakia (~40%), speckled leucoplakia (~30%), and chronic hyperplastic candidiasis. Erythroplakia has the HIGHEST malignant potential of all oral premalignant lesions.
Q10Carcinoma of the tongue most commonly occurs atW-17
✓ bLateral border of anterior 2/3 — Repeat of Q3. The lateral border of anterior 2/3 = the oral tongue. This is where tobacco chewing causes maximum mucosal damage and where SCC is most common. The tip and dorsum are much rarer sites.
Q11All are carcinogens for cancer of head and neck EXCEPTS-18
✓ dUnfluorinated water — Fluoride deficiency causes dental caries but is NOT a carcinogen for head and neck cancer. Established head and neck carcinogens: tobacco (smoking and chewing), alcohol (synergistic with tobacco), betel nut/areca nut, HPV (types 16 and 18), EBV (nasopharyngeal carcinoma), radiation exposure.
Q12Feature of tumour lysis syndrome isW-19
✓ cAlkalosis — Wait, the correct metabolic feature of tumour lysis syndrome (TLS) is ACIDOSIS (from lactic acid + uric acid), NOT alkalosis. The book marks "c) Alkalosis" which appears incorrect clinically. TLS features: HYPERuricaemia, HYPERphosphataemia, HYPERkalaemia, HYPOcalcaemia, and metabolic ACIDOSIS. For MUHS exam follow book answer: c. Clinically remember TLS = "PUKE": Phosphate↑, Uric acid↑, K↑, sEcalcium↓.
Clinical correction: TLS causes metabolic ACIDOSIS, not alkalosis. Hyperkalaemia, hyperphosphataemia, hyperuricaemia, hypocalcaemia. Book answer (c = Alkalosis) appears to be an error.
Q13Painless ulcer of tongue is due toW-19
✓ bSyphilis — Syphilitic tongue ulcer (primary chancre or gumma) is typically PAINLESS. Primary syphilitic chancre = painless, indurated, clean-based ulcer. Gumma (tertiary syphilis) = painless deep ulcer with punched-out edge. TB ulcers = painful (undermined edge). Carcinoma can be painless early but becomes painful. The classic PAINLESS tongue ulcer = syphilis.
Q14What is the diagnosis of Barium X-ray showing rat-tail appearance?PG 2020
✓ bCa oesophagus (irregular rat-tail = carcinoma) — Barium swallow X-ray patterns: IRREGULAR rat-tail/apple-core narrowing = carcinoma oesophagus. REGULAR/smooth rat-tail/bird-beak appearance = Achalasia cardia. Plummer-Wilson = post-cricoid web (upper oesophagus). The note in the book confirms: irregular = Ca oesophagus; regular = Achalasia (bird beak).
CH 50 Disorders of the Salivary Glands
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions (27 Questions)
Q1Commonest salivary gland tumour isM-03
✓ bPleomorphic adenoma — Pleomorphic adenoma (mixed salivary tumour) is the most common salivary gland tumour overall (~60–80% of all salivary gland tumours). Most common in the parotid gland. Features: slow-growing, painless, firm, lobulated, mobile. Risk of malignant transformation: ~5% → carcinoma ex-pleomorphic adenoma if left untreated.
Q2Stones are most common in which gland?M-03
✓ aSubmandibular gland — Submandibular gland stones (sialolithiasis) account for ~80–90% of all salivary calculi. Reasons: (1) Wharton's duct is long, runs uphill, has a wide opening, (2) Submandibular saliva is more alkaline, mucoid, and calcium-rich, (3) Duct has an upward curve against gravity. Parotid stones = only ~10–15%.
Q3Sjögren's syndrome refers to disease ofN-03
✓ aParotid gland — Sjögren's syndrome is an autoimmune exocrinopathy primarily affecting salivary (especially parotid) and lacrimal glands → xerostomia (dry mouth) + keratoconjunctivitis sicca (dry eyes). The parotid gland is characteristically enlarged bilaterally. Associated with rheumatoid arthritis, SLE, anti-Ro/SSA and anti-La/SSB antibodies.
Q4In submandibular gland surgery, nerve LEAST likely to be injured isM-04
✓ bHypoglossal nerve — During submandibular gland excision, nerves at risk: (1) Lingual nerve (superficial, most commonly injured — sensation to anterior 2/3 tongue), (2) Marginal mandibular branch of facial nerve (motor to depressors of lower lip — superficial, frequently injured), (3) Hypoglossal nerve (lies deep to gland — least likely to be injured). The PG entrance 2021 answer (Q27) also confirms lingual = most commonly injured.
Q5Salivary stones occur most commonly inN-04, N-09, M-10
✓ bSubmandibular gland — Repeat of Q2. Submandibular = 80–90% of all salivary calculi. Clinical: post-meal swelling + pain (obstructive sialadenitis), bimanual palpation of floor of mouth, plain X-ray (80% are radio-opaque), sialography, ultrasound.
Q6Functioning parotid tumour is usuallyN-04
✓ aBenign and Single — Warthin's tumour (adenolymphoma) is the only "functioning" (technetium-99m scanning shows uptake) benign parotid tumour. It is classically benign, can be bilateral (~10%), and occurs predominantly in older male smokers. Pleomorphic adenoma (most common) is also benign but does not take up technetium.
Q7Parotid duct isN-04
✓ bStenson's duct — Parotid duct = Stensen's duct (opens into the oral cavity opposite the upper 2nd molar). Submandibular duct = Wharton's duct (opens at sublingual caruncle on floor of mouth). Sublingual duct = Bartholin's duct (opens with Wharton's duct). Pancreatic duct = Duct of Wirsung.
Q8The submandibular salivary gland is drained byN-10
✓ cWharton's duct — Submandibular gland = Wharton's duct. Opens at the sublingual caruncle (papilla) on the floor of the mouth, lateral to the frenulum. Runs forward and medial under the mucosa of the floor of mouth, passing around the posterior border of mylohyoid.
Q9Treatment of choice for pleomorphic adenoma of parotid gland isN-10
✓ aSuperficial parotidectomy — Pleomorphic adenoma of parotid = SUPERFICIAL PAROTIDECTOMY (for superficial lobe tumours — where most occur). Enucleation has >40% recurrence (tumour has pseudopod extensions beyond capsule). Total parotidectomy is for deep lobe or malignant tumours. Superficial parotidectomy with facial nerve preservation is the gold standard.
Q10Lingual nerve, hypoglossal nerve and facial artery and vein are important surgical anatomy relations ofM-11
✓ bSubmandibular salivary gland — These are the critical surgical anatomy relations around the submandibular gland: Lingual nerve (above gland, loops around Wharton's duct), Hypoglossal nerve (below gland, medial), Marginal mandibular branch of facial nerve (superficial to the gland), Facial artery and vein (pass through the gland substance).
Q11Common causes of parotid swelling are EXCEPTW-11
✓ dAscending sialadenitis in elderly patient — "Ascending sialadenitis in elderly" = ascending infection due to poor oral hygiene/dehydration/reduced salivary flow in elderly — this IS actually a common cause of acute bacterial parotitis in elderly/post-op patients. The book marks this as EXCEPT. More likely the "Tracuna" (trauma — option b, appears to be a typo for "Trauma") is the exception. Follow book answer: d for exam.
Q12Highest likelihood of a tumour being malignant in salivary glands is fromW-12
✓ bMinor salivary glands — The SMALLER the salivary gland, the HIGHER the proportion of malignant tumours. Minor salivary glands: ~50–80% malignant. Sublingual: ~50% malignant. Submandibular: ~50% malignant. Parotid: only ~20–25% malignant (but largest volume of tumours). Rule: "The smaller the gland, the more likely the tumour is malignant."
Q13The most common cause of parotid swelling isS-13
✓ bPleomorphic adenoma — Pleomorphic adenoma is the most common cause of a UNILATERAL parotid swelling/lump (neoplastic). Bilateral parotid swelling: mumps, Sjögren's, sarcoidosis, alcoholic liver disease. Overall (including non-neoplastic causes): mumps is most common inflammatory cause, but for a clinic lump referral, pleomorphic adenoma is most common.
Q14Salivary gland calculi areS-13
✓ cEasily palpable by fingers (bimanual palpation) — Submandibular calculi in the duct (Wharton's duct on floor of mouth) can be EASILY PALPATED bimanually (finger in floor of mouth + external palpation). They are NOT most common in parotid (submandibular = most common). NOT associated with renal calculi. NOT diagnosed by lacrimal duct probe.
Q15Treatment of small benign tumor of superficial lobe of parotid isW-13
✓ bSuperficial parotidectomy — Repeat of Q9. Even small benign tumours (pleomorphic adenoma) require superficial parotidectomy, NOT enucleation. Enucleation leaves microscopically positive margins and has >40% local recurrence rate. Superficial parotidectomy with facial nerve identification and preservation is the minimum appropriate surgery.
Q16Most common salivary gland affected by neoplasmsS-14
✓ aParotid — The parotid gland is the most commonly involved salivary gland in NEOPLASMS (both benign and malignant) due to its large size. ~70–80% of all salivary gland neoplasms occur in the parotid. However, the PROPORTION of malignant tumours is lowest in the parotid (~20%) compared to other glands.
Q17Sialography is contraindicated inW-14
✓ cAcute parotitis — Sialography (contrast injection into salivary duct for imaging) is CONTRAINDICATED in ACUTE INFECTION because: (1) injection is painful, (2) contrast may worsen infection, (3) duct cannulation risks bacteraemia. Sialography IS indicated for: ductal calculi, chronic/recurrent sialadenitis, ductal strictures, and Sjögren's evaluation. Perform only when acute infection has resolved.
Q18In which head and neck cancer is perineural invasion most commonly seen?W-14
✓ bAdenoid cystic carcinoma — Adenoid cystic carcinoma (AdCC) of the salivary glands is notorious for PERINEURAL INVASION — it spreads along nerve sheaths (perineural invasion) causing pain and nerve palsies. Features: slow-growing, multiple recurrences, late distant metastases (lungs), poor long-term prognosis. Most common malignant tumour of submandibular and minor salivary glands.
Q19What is Ranula?S-15
✓ aRetention cyst of sublingual gland — Ranula = translucent, bluish-tinged swelling on the floor of the mouth = RETENTION CYST (or extravasation cyst) of the SUBLINGUAL gland. Looks like a frog's belly (Latin: rana = frog). Plunging ranula = when it extends through the mylohyoid muscle into the neck. Treatment: marsupialization or excision with sublingual gland.
Q20Most common tumour of parotid gland isW-15, S-18
✓ bPleomorphic adenoma — Repeat of Q1 and Q13. Pleomorphic adenoma = most common parotid tumour. 2nd most common benign parotid tumour: Warthin's tumour (adenolymphoma). Most common malignant parotid tumour: mucoepidermoid carcinoma (low/intermediate grade) or adenoid cystic carcinoma.
Q21Commonest salivary gland to get stone isW-15
✓ bSubmandibular gland — Repeat. Submandibular = 80–90% of all salivary stones.
Q22Sialolithiasis is more common in which salivary gland?S-16
✓ cSubmandibular gland — Repeat confirmation. Submandibular calculi are most common due to: long, tortuous, gravity-opposing duct; alkaline, mucoid, calcium-rich saliva; narrow bend at posterior mylohyoid.
Q23Starch iodine test is used to clinically demonstrate symptoms ofW-15
✓ bFrey's syndrome (auriculotemporal nerve syndrome) — Frey's syndrome = gustatory sweating after parotidectomy. Aberrant regeneration of parasympathetic secretomotor fibres (from auriculotemporal nerve) → innervate sweat glands of overlying skin → sweating/flushing when eating. Starch-iodine test (Minor's test): apply iodine to skin, dust with starch → area turns blue on sweating during eating.
Q24All help in localising facial nerve in parotid gland surgery EXCEPTW-17
✓ dInternal jugular vein — The IJV is not used as a landmark for facial nerve identification in parotidectomy. Landmarks for finding the facial nerve trunk: (1) Tragal pointer (1 cm deep and inferior to tragal cartilage), (2) Posterior belly of digastric muscle (nerve lies just superficial to it), (3) Mastoid tip, (4) Tympanomastoid suture line, (5) Nerve stimulator. IJV is a neck landmark, not parotid surgery landmark.
Q25Duct of submandibular salivary gland is known asS-19
✓ bWharton's duct — Repeat of Q8. Submandibular = Wharton's duct. Parotid = Stensen's duct. Sublingual = Bartholin's duct (major). Pancreatic = Duct of Wirsung. Santorini's duct = accessory pancreatic duct.
Q26Commonest salivary gland to get stone isW-19
✓ bSubmandibular — Fourth time this appears. Submandibular = the definitive answer for salivary gland stones. Memorise: 80–90% submandibular, ~10–15% parotid, <5% sublingual/minor.
Q27Most common nerve injured during submandibular gland surgery?PG 2021
✓ aLingual nerve — The lingual nerve (sensory to anterior 2/3 tongue) is the most commonly injured nerve during submandibular gland excision. It loops around Wharton's duct ("hooks" around the duct) and must be identified and preserved. Injury → numbness of ipsilateral anterior 2/3 tongue. The marginal mandibular branch is also at risk (motor to lower lip).

CH 51 The Thyroid and Parathyroid Glands
MCQ — Multiple Choice Questions
Q1Symptoms of thyrotoxicosis are controlled most rapidly byM-03
✓ cPropranolol — Beta-blockers (propranolol) control the SYMPATHOMIMETIC SYMPTOMS of thyrotoxicosis MOST RAPIDLY: tachycardia, tremor, anxiety, sweating, palpitations — within hours. Propyl-thiouracil and carbimazole take weeks to reduce thyroid hormone synthesis. Potassium iodide (Lugol's solution) reduces vascularity pre-operatively within days. Propranolol = fastest symptomatic relief.
Q2Hypoparathyroidism is seen afterM-04
✓ dThyroidectomy — Hypoparathyroidism (→ hypocalcaemia → tetany) is a well-known complication of thyroidectomy due to inadvertent removal or devascularisation of the parathyroid glands. Also occurs after block dissection of neck (radical neck dissection). Parathyroid insufficiency typically manifests 2–5 days post-operatively (when calcium stores are depleted).
Q3Carbimazole in hyperthyroidism acts byM-04
✓ dInhibition of iodination of tyrosine — Carbimazole (and propylthiouracil = PTU) inhibit thyroid peroxidase enzyme → prevent iodination of tyrosine residues on thyroglobulin → reduce T3/T4 synthesis. PTU ADDITIONALLY inhibits peripheral conversion of T4→T3. Carbimazole does NOT affect T4 release. Takes 4–6 weeks for effect (pre-formed hormone stores must deplete).
Q4Most common thyroid carcinoma isM-04
✓ aPapillary carcinoma — Papillary thyroid carcinoma (PTC) is the most common thyroid malignancy (~80–85%). Features: most common in young females, tends to spread via lymphatics (cervical lymph nodes), excellent prognosis, "orphan Annie eye nuclei," psammoma bodies on histology, associated with radiation exposure. BRAF V600E mutation in ~60%.
Q5Thyroid lymphoma is treated byN-08
✓ dChemoradiation — Primary thyroid lymphoma (usually diffuse large B-cell lymphoma or MALT lymphoma) is treated with CHEMORADIATION (combined chemotherapy + radiotherapy). Surgery has a limited role — only for diagnosis (core biopsy) or emergency airway decompression. Associated with Hashimoto's thyroiditis (risk of lymphoma is 50–70× higher). CHOP regimen + radiotherapy.
Q6The commonest cause of hyperparathyroidism isN-09
✓ cSingle gland hyperplasia — Wait, the book marks "c) Single gland hyperplasia" but the clinically correct answer is SINGLE PARATHYROID ADENOMA (~80–85% of primary hyperparathyroidism). Multigland hyperplasia = ~15%. The book answer appears incorrect. For MUHS: follow book (single gland hyperplasia = c). For clinical practice: single adenoma is by far the most common cause.
Clinical correction: Single parathyroid adenoma = 80–85% of primary hyperparathyroidism. Book marks "single gland hyperplasia" — this appears to be an error. Remember for PG exams: adenoma is most common.
Q7Commonest presentation of medullary carcinoma of thyroid isM-10
✓ aDiarrhoea — Medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC) arises from parafollicular C-cells that secrete CALCITONIN. Diarrhoea is the most common functional symptom (from calcitonin, prostaglandins, and VIP secretion causing hypermotility). Other features: neck mass, cervical lymphadenopathy, flushing. Elevated serum calcitonin = tumour marker for MTC. Associated with MEN 2A (RET proto-oncogene mutation).
Q8Parathyroid insufficiency following thyroid surgery most commonly presentsM-10, N-10
✓ b2–5 days — Post-thyroidectomy hypocalcaemia typically manifests 24–72 hours (most commonly 2–5 days) after surgery, when pre-existing calcium stores are depleted and remaining parathyroid glands (if accidentally devascularised) fail to compensate. Transient hypoparathyroidism is more common (~10%); permanent hypoparathyroidism is less common (~1%).
Q9Thyroid carcinoma which produces high level of serum calcitonin isN-10
✓ cMedullary carcinoma — MTC arises from C-cells (parafollicular cells) that produce CALCITONIN. Serum calcitonin is the specific tumour marker for MTC. Calcitonin level is used for: diagnosis, post-operative follow-up, and family screening. Stimulation test (pentagastrin or calcium) elevates calcitonin in MTC and in gene carriers (RET mutation).
Q10The normal range of free T4 isM-11
✓ a10–30 pmol/litre — Normal free T4 (fT4) = 10–25 pmol/L (approximately). The book states 10–30 pmol/litre. Normal free T3 = 3.5–7.5 pmol/litre. TSH normal = 0.4–4.0 mIU/L. Hyperthyroidism: ↑T4, ↑T3, ↓TSH. Hypothyroidism: ↓T4, ↓T3, ↑TSH.
Q11Following are the clinical types of thyrotoxicosis EXCEPTW-11
✓ aThyroid cancer — Thyroid cancer does NOT typically cause thyrotoxicosis (differentiated thyroid carcinomas are hormonally non-functional in most cases). Clinical types of thyrotoxicosis: (1) Diffuse toxic goitre (Graves' disease), (2) Toxic nodule (Plummer's disease), (3) Toxic multinodular goitre, (4) Thyroiditis (transient), (5) Exogenous thyroid hormone ingestion. Cancer ≠ thyrotoxicosis.
Q12Absolute indication for surgery in hyperparathyroidism are all EXCEPTW-11
✓ dLow serum calcium level — LOW calcium is NOT an indication for hyperparathyroidism surgery (it is the opposite of what occurs!). Hyperparathyroidism causes HIGH calcium. Absolute indications for parathyroidectomy in primary HPT: serum Ca >2.8 mmol/L (or >0.25 mmol/L above normal), renal calculi/nephrocalcinosis, marked bone density reduction (T-score <–2.5), age <50, symptoms (fatigue, weakness, depression, polyuria).
Q13Medullary carcinoma of the thyroid arises fromS-12
✓ aParafollicular cells (C-cells) — MTC arises from PARAFOLLICULAR C-CELLS (not follicular cells). C-cells are derived from neural crest and are scattered within the thyroid parenchyma. They produce calcitonin. This explains why MTC: (1) is not iodine-avid (no RAI uptake), (2) produces calcitonin, (3) is associated with MEN 2A/2B (RET mutations affecting neural crest derivatives).
Thyroid Cancer Quick Reference
📌Papillary (80–85%) = Most common, lymphatic spread, orphan Annie nuclei, psammoma bodies, BRAF mutation, best prognosis
📌Follicular (10–15%) = Haematogenous spread (bone/lung), capsular/vascular invasion, RAS/PAX8-PPARγ mutation
📌Medullary (~5%) = C-cells, calcitonin marker, MEN 2A/2B, RET mutation, diarrhoea, NO RAI uptake
📌Anaplastic (<1%) = Worst prognosis, elderly, rapid growth, compressive symptoms, no specific treatment

🔑 Head & Neck High-Yield Summary

  • Thyroglossal cyst: SUBHYOID most common (65–75%). Moves with tongue protrusion. Treatment: Sistrunk operation
  • Branchial cyst: 95% from 2nd arch. Anterior border SCM. Contains cholesterol crystals
  • Cystic hygroma: Posterior triangle. Brilliantly transilluminant. Treatment: surgical excision or sclerotherapy
  • Trotter's triad: NPC → deafness + soft palate immobility + trigeminal neuralgia
  • Carotid body tumour: "Lyre sign" on angiography. Compression between ICA and ECA
  • Radical neck dissection: removes SCM, IJV, CN XI + all lymph nodes. Vagus nerve always preserved
  • MRND Type III: preserves IJV + CN XI + SCM. Lymph nodes all removed
  • Cervical lymph nodes: 5 levels (Robbins classification)
  • Cleft lip repair: 3–6 months (Rule of Tens). Cleft palate: 9–18 months

🦷 Salivary Gland High-Yield

  • Parotid duct = Stensen's. Submandibular = Wharton's. Sublingual = Bartholin's
  • Stones: 80–90% submandibular. Parotid stones = only 10–15%
  • Most common tumour: Pleomorphic adenoma. Treatment = superficial parotidectomy (NOT enucleation)
  • Malignancy risk: Minor > Sublingual > Submandibular > Parotid
  • Adenoid cystic carcinoma = most common malignant minor salivary gland tumour = perineural invasion
  • Frey's syndrome = gustatory sweating post-parotidectomy. Diagnosed by Minor's starch-iodine test
  • Sialography CONTRAINDICATED in acute infection
  • Most common nerve injured in submandibular surgery = Lingual nerve
  • Sjögren's syndrome = parotid + lacrimal glands. Anti-Ro/SSA, anti-La/SSB antibodies
  • Ranula = retention cyst sublingual gland. "Plunging ranula" = extends into neck

🦋 Thyroid High-Yield

  • Most common thyroid cancer = Papillary. Most common in young women. Lymph spread. Psammoma bodies
  • Medullary cancer = C-cells = calcitonin = diarrhoea = MEN 2A/2B = RET mutation. NO RAI uptake
  • Propranolol = fastest symptom control. Carbimazole = inhibits thyroid peroxidase = blocks iodination
  • Post-thyroidectomy hypocalcaemia = 2–5 days. Signs: Trousseau's + Chvostek's. Give IV calcium gluconate
  • Thyroid lymphoma = Chemoradiation. Associated with Hashimoto's thyroiditis
  • Primary hyperparathyroidism: most common cause = single adenoma (80%). Book says single gland hyperplasia
  • Indication to NOT operate: LOW serum calcium (opposite of HPT). Operate when Ca >2.8 mmol/L, renal stones, bone loss
